Deep Learning: AI's Bias and Limitations
--- Page 45 ---
Deep Learning and the Black Box Problem Deep learning uses neural networks to map variables to outcomes in complex ways Intricate layers and connections in neural network makes it difficult to precisely determine the relationship between inputs and outcomes Lack of transparency raises concerns about accountability and trust Models achieve high accuracy but are complex and hard to understand Pathways from input to output are often obscured
--- Page 46 ---
AI’s Biases AI, like humans, is susceptible to bias, which can be amplified by processing large data sets Susceptibility to Bias Sources of Bias Arises from missing or incomplete data Black Box Nature Humans may not fully understand how these models work, which complicates the bias problem Difficulty in Addressing Bias Foundational issues such as measurement error and data dependencies, can lead to biased outcomes

Addressing Bias in AI Inclusive Data Training: Ensure data represents all populations Explainable AI: Develop models that explain decision - making processes Regular Bias Testing: Continuously test for and mitigate biases Human Oversight: Maintain human accountability for AI decisions and ensure responsible use

Prediction Vs Causation • Managers need to understand that AI predictions are not always causal • AI excels at predicting behavior rather than explaining why it occurs • Example: AI can predict your next purchases or an employee's likelihood of leaving, but not the reasons behind these predictions
--- Page 49 ---
Example: Insurance Segment One Segment Two • Uses health services more, indicating higher need for insurance • Hasty conclusion might lead to focusing on marketing efforts while ignoring potential root causes • Uses healthcare services less, possibly suggesting a lower need for health insurance • Use less healthcare due to affordability concerns, making them a target for insurance products
--- Page 50 ---
Prediction Vs Causation Managers must be cautious when acting on AI predictions Always dig deeper to understand the reasons Identify the root causes to make informed business decisions
--- Page 51 ---
Predictions Not Being Casual In the case of the health - care algorithm, higher rates of illness (X) were correctly correlated with higher health - care costs (Y) for segment 1 However, for segment 2, higher rates of illness did not in general lead to higher hospital spending It is plausible that X depends on Z (income) which may not have been considered, known as the omitted variable bias AI found a correlation: high illness rates (X) mean higher healthcare costs (Y). However, X doesn't necessarily cause Y.
--- Page 52 ---
Correlation Vs Causation Simultaneity Selection Bias • Arises when the training data is not representative of the world population • Example : Excluding people without formal healthcare skews illness predictions for the broader population • Occurs when two variables are influenced by a third, unseen factor • Example: higher hospital prices and illness rates might both be influenced by poverty
