This Coupang role is a strong fit, but the interview narrative must be carefully translated from **telecom/platform leadership** into **high-scale, business-critical backend platform leadership**.

The role is not looking for a pure people manager. It expects a Director who can:

- Build and scale engineering teams.
- Provide technical direction for scalable, extendable, fault-tolerant systems.
- Partner with Product, Business, and Engineering leaders.
- Own operational excellence metrics.
- Drive mission-critical initiatives.
- Hire experienced ICs and develop managers/technical leaders.
- Operate with urgency, ownership, and hands-on architectural judgment.

I have led 60+ member engineering organizations, supported platforms handling 700M+ daily transactions, delivered 35% cloud cost optimization, improved platform performance by 200%, and commercialized global telecom platforms across 40+ operators. ## 6. Main Interview Risks and Mitigation

### Risk 1: No direct e-commerce / commerce platform background

**Likely challenge:**

> You have telecom experience. Why should we believe you can lead backend engineering for Coupang?

**Response strategy:**

- Do not apologize for domain difference.
- Translate telecom scale into commerce-relevant platform leadership.
- Show how you learn new domains quickly.
- Explain a 30/60/90-day operating model.

**Suggested answer:**

> That is a fair question. My direct domain has been telecom and enterprise platforms, but the leadership pattern maps well to Coupang’s needs: high transaction volume, production reliability, distributed systems, cost pressure, stakeholder complexity, and customer-impacting delivery. I have repeatedly operated platforms where downtime, latency, and delivery delays had direct business impact. In the first 30 days, I would learn Coupang’s commerce operating model, critical customer journeys, platform architecture, and operational metrics. Then I would identify the top reliability, scalability, cost, and delivery bottlenecks and build a focused execution plan with Product, Business, and Engineering leaders.

### Risk 2: Sounding too operational instead of Director-level

**Mitigation:**

Every answer must include:

1. Business context.
2. Scope of ownership.
3. Leadership decision.
4. Trade-off.
5. Execution model.
6. Metric impact.
7. Leadership lesson.

### Risk 3: Too much telecom jargon

**Mitigation:**

Convert internal terminology into platform language:

| Avoid | Use instead |
|---|---|
| Telecom systems | Mission-critical transaction platforms |
| eSIM/RCS only | Global platform commercialization |
| Internal system names | Sales, customer, provisioning, billing, and integration platforms |
| AI adoption | AI-assisted SDLC, test automation, incident analysis, productivity improvement |

### Risk 4: Hands-on architecture expectation

The JD expects hands-on architecture experience with scalable products/services, Java stack, AWS cloud, and big data.

Prepare to go deep on:

- Distributed system design.
- Fault tolerance.
- Scalability bottlenecks.
- API design and platform boundaries.
- Data consistency and eventual consistency.
- Observability.
- Cloud cost and performance trade-offs.
- Monolith-to-platform modernization.
- Incident prevention.
- Architecture governance across multiple teams.

## 8. Interview Story Bank

### Story 1: 700M+ daily transaction platform

**Use for:** Scale, reliability, backend systems, operational excellence.

**Director-level framing:**

> I owned engineering outcomes for a mission-critical platform processing 700M+ daily transactions. The business challenge was not just throughput; it was reliability, latency, operational visibility, and change safety. I focused the teams on architecture stability, observability, release governance, and performance bottlenecks. The outcome was stronger platform reliability and measurable performance improvement.

**Prepare metrics:**

- Daily transaction volume.
- Latency or throughput improvement.
- Incident reduction if available.
- Availability/SLA if available.
- Team size and stakeholder scope.

### Story 2: 35% cloud cost optimization

**Use for:** Growth/profitability, Hate Waste, business-aware engineering.

**Director-level framing:**

> Under budget pressure, I led a cloud cost optimization initiative without treating it as a blind cost-cutting exercise. We looked at usage patterns, right-sizing, waste, architecture inefficiencies, and engineering ownership. The result was 35% optimization while protecting reliability and delivery commitments.

**Prepare metrics:**

- Baseline cloud spend.
- Optimization levers.
- Trade-offs rejected.
- Governance model.
- Business impact.

### Story 3: Global eSIM commercialization across 40+ operators

**Use for:** Mission-critical delivery, global stakeholders, platform commercialization.

**Director-level framing:**

> I led platform engineering for a global eSIM commercialization effort across 40+ operators. The complexity was not only technical; it involved interoperability, stakeholder alignment, rollout governance, and business readiness. I created an execution model that balanced platform standardization with market-specific needs.

**Prepare metrics:**

- Operators/markets covered.
- Platform capabilities delivered.
- Launch timeline.
- Business or adoption impact.
- Stakeholder groups involved.

### Story 4: 200% platform performance improvement

**Use for:** Architecture, technical direction, Dive Deep, Simplify.

**Director-level framing:**

> I led a platform performance improvement initiative where the first step was to isolate whether the constraint was architecture, database, integration, or operational process. We avoided superficial tuning and addressed the system bottlenecks directly. The result was 200% performance improvement and better scalability headroom.

**Prepare metrics:**

- Baseline performance.
- Bottleneck discovered.
- Architecture decision.
- Trade-off made.
- Result.

### Story 5: AI-assisted engineering adoption

**Use for:** Modern engineering leadership, productivity, developer experience.

**Director-level framing:**

> I drove AI-assisted engineering adoption not as a tool rollout, but as an operating model change. We identified practical use cases in unit testing, integration testing, documentation, production log investigation, backlog analysis, and engineering knowledge discovery. The focus was measurable productivity, quality, and operational improvement.

**Prepare metrics:**

- Adoption percentage.
- Test coverage improvement.
- Cycle-time reduction.
- Defect leakage reduction.
- Time saved in analysis or documentation.

## 11. 30/60/90-Day Plan

### First 30 days — Learn and diagnose

- Understand Coupang’s business model, customer journeys, platform architecture, and operational metrics.
- Meet Product, Business, Engineering, TPM, SRE, Data, and India/global stakeholders.
- Review team topology, ownership boundaries, incident history, delivery predictability, and technical debt.
- Identify top reliability, scalability, cost, and productivity pain points.
- Build trust by listening before changing.

**Output by day 30:** Current-state assessment with top 5 risks/opportunities.

### First 60 days — Prioritize and align

- Define operational excellence metrics for owned systems.
- Identify 2–3 high-leverage initiatives: reliability, cost, delivery, architecture simplification, or developer productivity.
- Establish review mechanisms for incidents, architecture, delivery, and engineering quality.
- Identify leadership gaps and talent development needs.
- Align roadmap with Product and Business stakeholders.

**Output by day 60:** Prioritized execution roadmap with measurable outcomes.

### First 90 days — Execute and show traction

- Deliver one visible operational improvement.
- Launch one architecture simplification or reliability initiative.
- Improve one delivery/productivity metric.
- Establish manager/tech-lead development cadence.
- Build a longer-term platform modernization plan.

**Output by day 90:** Measurable early wins and a 6-month engineering plan.
