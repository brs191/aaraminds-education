## Introduction to Critical Thinking

### Evolution of Critical Thinking *(Page)*

Critical thinking is often cited as an essential twenty-first-century skill, but its origins trace back to 1910 with John Dewey, an American philosopher, psychologist, and educator. Dewey defined it as "active, persistent, and careful consideration of any belief or supposed form of knowledge in the light of the grounds that support it, and the further conclusions to which it tends."

David Moore, author of *Critical Thinking and Intelligence Analysis*, modernized the definition, describing critical thinking as a deliberate meta-cognitive (thinking about thinking) and cognitive (thinking) act whereby a person reflects on the quality of the reasoning process while simultaneously reasoning to a conclusion. The thinker has two equally important goals: reaching a solution and improving the way they reason.

Critical thinking is vital for effective leadership — it provides a framework of reflection, structured reasoning, and intuitive thinking, and helps dismiss false information, avoid incorrect conclusions, and avoid decisions based on emotion rather than reason. This segment introduces critical thinking alongside decision-making and related concepts such as biases, deductions, premises, and conclusions, plus a case study on JumpToPC.

*Reference: Moore, D. T. (2010). Critical Thinking and Intelligence Analysis. Books Express Publishing.*

## Introduction to Syllogisms

**Video 1.4 (5:22): Syllogism** — Explores deductive reasoning, syllogisms, and fallacies. Deductive reasoning usually follows steps: a major premise, then a minor premise, then a conclusion. A syllogism is a form of deductive reasoning in which two statements (major and minor premise) reach a logical conclusion. Example: "All men are mortal; Socrates is a man; therefore Socrates is mortal." In the workforce, employees accept established premises and formulate approaches based on them — e.g., implementing a bi-weekly feedback program after research, then observing a 25% increase in job satisfaction and a 35% increase in sense of personal improvement, concluding the program boosted performance and well-being.

**Video 1.5 (6:25): Propositional Fallacies** — Propositional logic is a logical system for deducing valid compound propositions from simpler propositions. *Workbook prompt:* create one example of modus ponens and one of modus tollens.

**Video 1.6 (3:23): Quantification Fallacies** — Quantification uses terms such as *all, some, most, few, none*. The general rule: when an argument uses quantifiers in the premises, the conclusion's quantification must agree — generally, do not broaden the quantifier when reaching a conclusion. *Workbook prompt:* describe a time you made an incorrect assumption based on a premise.

## Thinking about Deductive Thinking

**Video 1.7 (6:37): Thinking about Deductive Thinking** — According to Jonathan Haber (author of *Critical Thinking*), deductive arguments are "self-contained" — everything needed to determine whether the conclusion is true can be found in the premises and the argument's form. "Valid" refers to a deductive argument that requires you to accept the conclusion as true if you accept the premises as true. A deductive, valid argument whose premises are actually true is said to be "sound." *Reference: Haber, J. (2020). Critical Thinking, The MIT Press Essential Knowledge Series.*

**Video 1.8 (6:35): More Thinking about Deductive Thinking** — If all premises are true and you use sound deductive reasoning with significant patterns, the conclusions you reach are necessarily true. *Workbook prompt:* describe a time you overcame your own bias.

**Video 1.9 (3:48): Deductive Reasoning – Summary** — Reviews the diagnostic abilities of deductive reasoning: finding faults in premises, mistakes in reason, and the limits of deductive reasoning (knowing when a situation is indeterminate). *Workbook prompt:* describe how you solved a problem in your organization.

### Required Assignment 1.1: Thinking about Thinking *(Assignment — 1 pt, due 2026-04-23, required)*

Learning outcomes: apply critical thinking and metacognition; analyze alternative solutions critically; identify evidence for or against alternative solutions.

**Instructions:** Academics and managers have long believed organizations must trade off between flexibility and efficiency. Contingency theory holds that organizations and their processes need to be designed to match the nature of their work; a common variable is the degree of uncertainty in the environment (often conceptualized as the need for innovation). When the competitive environment and work are stable and well understood, contingency theory suggests more highly structured, mechanistic designs; when facing highly uncertain situations requiring ongoing adaptation, more flexible, organic designs perform better.

Due to the COVID-19 pandemic, every organization had to adapt to remote/online work. Some organizations claim they can safely bring employees back; others say it is impossible to guarantee safety. In 400–500 words, answer: Which side of the argument do you think is right, and why? What evidence supports your claims? How did you come to a conclusion?

*Reference: D. Keiffer, N. P. Repenning, and J. Repenning (2018). A new approach to designing work.* Submission: upload a Word document; estimated time 30 minutes.
