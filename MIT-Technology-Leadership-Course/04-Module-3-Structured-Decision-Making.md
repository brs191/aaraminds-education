## Decision Making

### Introduction to Decision Making *(Page)*

This segment covers the research behind decision making: decision-making models, the psychology of decision making, situation awareness, and other factors. Decisions are a regular part of human life and strongly influence individuals and others depending on the decision maker's position. Understanding decision-making processes can help prevent bad decisions and stimulate good ones. Many factors influence decisions and their relationships are not fully known; decision making is approached from different points of view even within a single science. Contemporary decision-making theories in psychology offer more realistic views than classical ones — not demanding that humans behave like computers or irrational creatures — tracing development from the economic rational being and bounded rationality to naturalistic decision making.

*Reference: Polic, M. (2009). Decision making: Between rationality and reality. Interdisciplinary Description of Complex Systems 7(2):78-89.*

### Required Discussion 3.1: Group Decision Making *(Discussion — required, counts toward completion)*

Learning outcomes: apply principles of the Pugh Matrix and Evaluation Matrix; analyze the Pugh Matrix to identify opportunities for bias.

Background: There is no voting in the Pugh Method. When experts disagree about whether a concept is better than a datum, both sides discuss their reasons. This often resolves the issue because facts unknown to some experts come to light, or clarifications are made about what a design concept entails or what a criterion means.

- If discussion leads to agreement, a **+** or **−** is entered into the matrix.
- If disagreement persists, an **S** is entered. **S** can mean experts agree the concept's merit is similar to the datum, or that differences are controversial and cannot yet be determined — in which case team members are encouraged to gather additional information.

The evaluation matrix includes summary scores along the bottom (counts of + and − or S scores per concept) as a rough measure of each alternative's characteristics. These scores are sometimes mistakenly interpreted as a way to choose a single winning design (reflected in the common term "Pugh Concept Selection"), but Pugh emphasized "Controlled Convergence." The first run reduces the number of concepts; it is not meant to choose a single alternative. A matrix run can result in (non-mutually-exclusive) decisions to: eliminate weak concepts; invest in further development of some concepts; invest in information gathering; develop additional concepts based on what the matrix and discussions revealed. The matrix should be run iteratively as part of a convergence process. An Excel file containing the Evaluation Matrix is downloadable.

*Reference: Frey et al. (2009). The Pugh Controlled Convergence method: model-based evaluation and implications for design theory.*

**Scenario 1:** A senior director has four direct reports. One unit is five times the size of the others and is composed of three equally sized sub-units. The director of this large entity is retiring. A colleague proposes that instead of a search for a new director, the three sub-unit managers each report to the senior director — making six manageable direct reports and saving the retiring director's salary. *Question 1:* Analyze the argument. Using deductive thinking, identify the unstated premises that should be articulated and evaluated. Create a counter-argument with reasons to keep the current structure.

**Scenario 2:** Provost is a classic North American bike manufacturer producing commuter, mountain, and electric bikes. It has two options for manufacturing its latest product: one substantial plant making all bicycles in one location and shipping worldwide, or five plants each closer to points of sale. A colleague argues for five plants for three reasons: reduced shipping costs; more customer commitment because local plants support the local economy; greater resilience to disruptions. *Question 2:* You see the points but aren't convinced. What evidence would validate the five-plant argument? If you feel the single-plant option is better, argue your points and explain your reasoning.
