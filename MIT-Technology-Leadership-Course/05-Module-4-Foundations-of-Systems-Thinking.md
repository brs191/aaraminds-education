## Emergence

### Video 4.2 (4:51): Emergence *(Page)*
System thinkers view everything as a system — a set of interrelated entities that perform a function, where the functionality is greater than the sum of the parts. Four main ideas: there are entities; the entities are interrelated; the entities and relationships enable a function; the function is more powerful than the sum of the parts (emergence).

Four types of emergence: emergence of function (the system does something never done before); emergence of performance (the system does something better than before); emergence of the *ilities* / nonfunctional attributes (e.g., more reliability); emergence of an emergency (something we don't want to happen).

## Function

### Introduction to Function *(Page)*
Consider a television's function — to entertain, decorate a room, display an image, transmit information. The perceived function of a device depends on context (a television in a contemporary living room vs. a soccer match vs. a conference room). Define the television's function using system thinking terminology.

### Video 4.3 (4:08): Function *(Page)*
Introduces the first task of system thinking — identify the system, its form, and its function. Function is what a system does: the activities, operations, and transformations that cause or contribute to performance. A function's description has two parts: the change itself (the process) and the thing changed or operated on (the operand). Function is how systems create benefit; emergence occurs in the functional domain.

## Form

### Introduction to Form *(Page)*
Consider riding a bike in different ways — a hybrid bike, handcycling, a unicycle. All three share a common emergent primary function (transport, entertain, or exercise the rider) but their form (one, two, or three wheels) differs greatly.

### Video 4.4 (4:49): Form *(Page)*
Form is what the system **is** — the physical or informational embodiment, involving material, configuration, text, and instructions. Form is the instrument of function. Value is benefit at cost: benefit derives from function, cost depends on form. Abstractions make systems appear simple by hiding unnecessary details.

### Self-Study Poll 4.3 *(Page)*
You are replacing an old television; you create an abstraction of relevant features for product selection. Abstraction hides unnecessary details; the relevant features depend on the purchaser's point of view. Features split into Form (Cost, Size, Source Inputs, Color) and Function (Power Consumption, Resolution, Refresh Rate, VESA Compatibility, Smart Features).

## Identifying Entities

### Video 4.5 (5:56): Identifying Entities *(Page)*
After identifying the system, its function, and its form, the second task is identifying the entities and their function and form. A system can be decomposed into entities and entities composed into a system; decomposition and composition are powerful tools for managing complexity. Every system is part of a larger system and can be decomposed into smaller ones; every system and entity can be thought of as an abstraction with form and function; systems and entities can be placed in a hierarchy based on form or function. *Workbook prompt:* read the "Identifying Entities" scenario "The Small Cabin" and specify the function for every component of a water system.

## System Boundaries

### Self-Study Try-It Activity 4.1: Problem Analysis *(Page)*
You plan to commute by bike (a 15-minute ride in traffic). As a system thinker you identify the form and function — bicycle (form) transports the rider (function = process + operand) — then identify the entities of the system and the system boundary.

### Video 4.6 (6:30): System Boundaries *(Page)*
Continues the second task — identifying entities, their function, and form — and defining the system boundary. Identify entities by decomposition or composition; limit the number of entities to 7 ± 2; use holistic thinking to identify all reasonable entities; focus on the minimum set of essential entities; draw a boundary dividing the system from its context.

### Self-Study Knowledge Check 4.5 *(Practice quiz, 2 questions, 0 pts)* — Learning outcomes: analyze a personal/professional activity using systems thinking; correlate relationships among entities with behavior and performance.

### Example: System Boundaries *(Page)*
A baker-entrepreneur team considers expanding a traditional bakery to a futuristic one and makes a system and context map. They start with three subsystems (acquire materials, make bread, sell bread), then stretch holistic thinking to identify three to five additional entities that could be part of the holistic system. ## Functional Relationships

### Introduction to Functional Relationships *(Page)*
For a simple bicycle system, the cyclist and ground are in the context; the system comprises the entities seat, frame, and wheels. Relationships between entities are essential — otherwise it is just a pile of parts:

| Between Entities | Relationship |
|---|---|
| Cyclist and Seat | The seat supports the cyclist |
| Cyclist and Frame | The cyclist propels the frame |
| Seat and Frame | The frame supports the seat |
| Frame and Wheels | The wheels support the frame |
| Wheels and Ground | The wheels roll on the ground |

Consider whether each relationship is based more on function or form.

### Video 4.7 (5:04): Identifying Relationships *(Page)*
The third task: identifying the relationships among the entities, their form, and their function. A group of entities becomes a system because of relationships — otherwise it is just a collection of independent entities. Relationships have form and function; emergence happens as a direct result of functional relationships. Functional relationships are interactions — one entity affects another and an operand is passed or shared; they can be among entities within the system or with entities outside in the context.

## Formal Relationships

### Required Discussion 4.2: Introduction to Formal Relationships *(Discussion — required, counts toward completion)*

David Thompson was president and CEO of Orbital ATK, a global leader in aerospace and defense technologies, before retiring in 2018. He cofounded Orbital Sciences Corp. in 1982 and served as chairman and CEO until its 2014 merger with Alliant Techsystems (ATK).

Extract: The **Pegasus rocket**, developed ~30 years ago, was conceived as a small satellite launch vehicle. Unlike prior ground-launched rockets, Pegasus was launched from a high-flying airplane, boosting its payload capacity and giving operational flexibility. Its system challenges were twofold: (1) the rocket itself was a complex system with new technology — an all-composite airframe, dozens of distributed microprocessors controlling aerodynamic surfaces and the thrust vector system; (2) it was a system in a bigger sense — it had to safely mate with a large carrier aircraft, be compatible with the intended class of small satellites, and operate within existing air traffic and launch range infrastructure in the US and overseas. Pegasus succeeded because its system elements were understood from the beginning and harmonized; it represented sales of over 200 rockets (~$6 billion in business) launched from a dozen sites worldwide.

**Discussion Prompt:** What issues of Pegasus system composition did Mr. Thompson discuss — were there issues of hierarchy or system boundaries? What issues of formal and functional relationships did he mention, and how did they contribute to Pegasus's success?

**Initial post:** 300–400 words. **Rubric:** 1 pt (Full Marks) — all prompts answered, 300–400 words, relevant; 0 pts — incomplete or no response.

### Video 4.8 (6:49): Structure *(Page)*
Continues the third task — identifying relationships among entities, their form and function. Functional relationships (interactions among entity functions) lead directly to emergence. Formal relationships between entities are called structures. Where there is a functional relationship there is usually a formal relationship; the functional interaction is usually enabled by a formal relationship. Structure includes connections, location, and sequence. When structure and interaction cross a system boundary, careful system thinking is required. *Workbook prompt:* analyze the formal relationships in the structure diagram of a home water system and discuss the importance of structure.

### Example: Formal Relationships *(Page)*
A formal relationship is structure — how one entity is connected to another, where they are located, or in what sequence. Each interaction is enabled by a structure:

| System | Pair of components | Interaction | Structure |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bicycle | Rider and seat | Exchange loads | Seat surface |
| Bicycle | Rider and traffic signal | Transmitting light | Signal visible to the rider |
| Court | Prosecutor and judge | Present evidence | Verbal in court |
| Court | Judge and defendant | Announce verdict | Verbal and document |
| Bakery | Storage and mixer | Transfer flour, yeast, etc. | Boxes |
| Bakery | Mixer and oven | Transfer dough | Bucket |
| Bakery | Salesperson and customer | Deliver bread | Tray |
| Bakery | Customer and salesperson | Make payment | Credit card and reader |
