## Form, Function, and Performance

### Video 5.1 (4:27): Form, Function, and Performance *(Page)*
Professor Balakrishnan examines logistics and transportation systems — ways to coordinate the movement of people, goods, and services across a complex network. The performance of a system depends on its entities, their relationships, our knowledge of the system, and our ability to regulate it. Performance can vary because of differences in form, entities, entity connectivity, scale, control of entities, knowledge of the system, or operand behavior.

### Video 5.2 (2:40): Form, Function, and Performance — Three Scenarios *(Page)*
Different systems can have the same function but give very different performance because our ability to control entities, our knowledge of the system, or operand behavior differs. Three scenarios are considered.

## Counterintuitive Behavior

### Video 5.3 (4:07): Counterintuitive Emergent Behavior *(Page)*
In complex systems, emergent behavior can be counterintuitive. We believe adding more resources always improves performance — e.g., adding a road to a congested network always decreases travel times — but in reality adding a road in the wrong place can worsen performance and increase travel times.

### Video 5.4 (5:22): Counterintuitive Emergent Behavior — Examples *(Page)*
Professor Balakrishnan discusses examples of counterintuitive emergent behavior and explores the Price of Anarchy — a concept in economics and game theory where system performance degrades because agents (operands) act in their own self-interest (selfish behavior). *Workbook prompt:* complete the activities for counterintuitive emergent behavior.

## The Bullwhip Effect

### Video 5.7 (5:19): The Bullwhip Effect *(Page)*
Considers what happens when interconnected entities make decisions in response to events around them. The bullwhip effect results from a trade-off between uncertainty and the efficiency of the system; improving information sharing and the ability to regulate the system can mitigate it.

## The Computational Approach

### Introduction to Computational Approach *(Page)*
Recall the four key tasks of systems thinking from Professor Crawley as Professor Daniela Rus presents the computational approach: think about a system as form and function (form is what it is, function is what it does); think about the entities (a form enabling their function); think about the relationships (the structure enabling the interactions); reason how the functions of entities interact to produce emergence and value.

### Video 5.9 (4:30): The Computational Approach to System Thinking *(Page)*
System thinking is problem-solving in complex settings. Systems are hierarchically composed of subsystems with increasingly simple functions toward the bottom of the hierarchy. A system is an entity with interrelated and interdependent parts, defined by its modules, the boundaries between modules, and their interaction. The computational systems thinking design process: **database → choose modules → compose modules → simulate and verify → fabricate.** *Workbook prompt:* complete the Reflection on the Computational Approach and identify the four entities in the example system.

## Case Studies

### Video 5.10 (5:15): Case Study — The Robot Compiler *(Page)*
A compiler takes a high-level specification and delivers a working system. With a database of designs, you can segment and compose that database to make new designs from existing ones. The computational approach is data-driven, relying on a database seeded with low-level designs and some higher-level designs created by experts.

### Video 5.11 (4:38): Case Study — The Database *(Page)*
The entities in a database are mechanical parts, electromechanical parts, and software components. All database components must be parameterized for significant representation and scalability. Computational approaches fill the gap between symbolic reasoning and concrete, precise specifications.
