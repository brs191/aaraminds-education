## Learning outcomes addressed

- Analyze a personal or professional activity using systems thinking.
- Identify the importance of form and function in decision making.
- Correlate important relationships among the entities of the systems with behavior and performance.
- Evaluate a selected system in the context of other systems with which it interacts.

## Instructions

Throughout Module 4 you apply a system thinking approach to a personal or professional activity system, building the assignment template incrementally across nine steps. Submit the completed template; it should meet the rubric criteria.

A **personal activity system** is one encountered outside your profession (a camera, a kitchen, a choral group). A **professional activity system** is one encountered at work (a piece of code, a professional education).

## The nine steps

| Step | Description |
|---|---|
| 1 | Your Personal Activity System — describe your personal or professional activity system. |
| 2 | The Magic of Your System — describe the emergent property; identify what is "magical" about it. |
| 3 | Apply Emergence to Your Professional Activity System — find an example based on a professional activity system. |
| 4 | Apply Function to Your Activity System — identify and describe the primary function and the benefit delivered. |
| 5 | Apply Form to Your Activity System — explain how you would use abstractions of form and function to describe the system to your CEO. |
| 6 | Apply Entities to Your Activity System — apply what you learned about entities. |
| 7 | Apply System Boundaries to Your Activity System — revisit composition/decomposition with holistic thinking and focus. |
| 8 | Apply Functional Relationships to Your Activity System — describe the important functional interactions. |
| 9 | Apply Formal Relationships to Your Activity System — describe three important formal relationships (structure) and how each enables interaction. |
