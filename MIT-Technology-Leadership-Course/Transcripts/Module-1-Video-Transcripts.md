## Video 1 (06:11) — Introduction to Critical Thinking

Welcome to the course Critical Thinking and Decision Making. I'm Dan Frey. I'm a Professor at MIT, and I'm going to be guiding you through this material. I think critical thinking and decision making is one of the most fascinating and really important topics I can think of. It has a big impact on engineering and business, and really, life in general.

Let me make a couple of observations to start with. The term critical thinking has a nearly unambiguously positive connotation, which is strange because the words "critical" and "thinking" don't really have that. Think about the word critical. There are people who serve as critics — art critics, food critics, movie critics — and people sometimes really don't uniformly praise those people. They get tired of being criticized because their job is to point out both the strengths and the weaknesses of their work, and that can become exasperating. Similarly, the word thinking is not unambiguously positive. There are expressions like "You're overthinking this." If action is what's needed at that time, maybe thinking is the wrong approach. But when you combine the two together, it denotes a concept that really is an underserved need. Nobody says, "Yeah, we've had enough of that critical thinking stuff." People really want more critical thinking, for good reasons.

It is useful to have a formal definition. This one is by an author named David T. Moore: "Critical thinking is a deliberate meta-cognitive (thinking about thinking) and cognitive (thinking) act whereby a person reflects on the quality of the reasoning process simultaneously while reasoning to a conclusion. The thinker has two equally important goals: coming to a solution and improving the way she or he reasons."

In the course, we'll spend a lot of time on the thinking part, but I want to make sure we give adequate emphasis to the meta-cognitive part as well — while you're trying to come to good conclusions, also think about what you're doing, and try to become better all the time.

There's a cloud of words in the graphic — concepts emphasized in this course. The highlighted terms are emphasized in week one. **Deduction** is a process of reasoning from premises to conclusions. We're also going to talk about hypotheses and how they're entertained and tested. To introduce that: imagine a universe with cards that always have letters on one side and numbers on the other. Four cards are dealt showing a D, an F, a three, and a seven. You're entertaining the hypothesis that if a card has a D on one side, it must have a three on the other side. You're allowed to turn over exactly two of the four cards. Which two would you choose?

I have done this exercise with many groups; the most common answers are the D and the three. I agree the D should be turned over, but I'm not sure the three is the best. If you turn over the D and a three shows up, that is some evidence confirming your hypothesis; if some other number shows up, like a four, you get definitive information that your hypothesis is wrong — it has been falsified. If you turn over the three you might see a D (some confirming evidence), but if you see another letter you learn nothing, because the hypothesis doesn't say what should be on the other side of a non-D card. If you turn over the seven and there's a D on the other side, you have gained definitive evidence that your hypothesis has been falsified. A meta-cognitive stance of being willing to expose your working hypotheses to potentially falsifying information tends to maximize information gained under constraints. Let's always put our positions out in the open and try to expose them to potentially falsifying information.

## Video 2 — Case Study (06:01) — JumpToPC

In this segment I introduce a case study that gives lots of examples we can use for critical thinking, called **JumpToPC**. A group of people working in India is launching a new product, and many decisions need to be made along the way — that's the nature of design: decision making with limited information.

JumpToPC is intended to be an affordable personal computing solution for low-income households in India. Partners include MIT D-Lab (an organization at MIT that seeks to improve the lives of people living in poverty) and Greenway (a manufacturer of appliances like cook stoves, local to the Indian context). The team includes Sucheta Balinga, Dan Sweeney, and myself. The project's main objective is to make digital literacy and usage of digital tools affordable, accessible, and adaptable. On affordability: the cheapest PC costs about 20,000 Indian rupees (~282 US dollars), which is not affordable because nine out of ten Indian adults earn less than 12,000 rupees a month. On accessibility: we want a user interface that leverages skills people already have. On adaptability: the solution should make use of things already in the environment of the people we're trying to serve.

Social context: Less than 10 percent of Indian households have broadband or Wi-Fi at home — people use Wi-Fi at places like Internet cafes. About 45 percent of Indian households have a smartphone, so this is very much a phones-first environment. One team member says most Indians can tap and swipe but few can type and click — because India went to smartphones first, people are accustomed to making videos, sharing on social media, browsing, but have not practiced editing a resume or working with an Excel spreadsheet. Another challenge: women and girls are sometimes not allowed to move freely outside the house, so bringing digital literacy tools to the household might be a good strategy.

The technology: JumpToPC uses an existing television (many Indian households have an older cathode-ray-tube TV) as a screen; a smartphone as the CPU, broadcasting via a wireless display adapter so the smartphone's screen is displayed in a larger format on the TV, with changed software so it looks more like a traditional desktop / Windows environment; and input devices like a wireless keyboard and mouse integrated by Bluetooth. The overall system — input, processing, display — can be made available for about one-fifth of what a laptop would cost. Greenway (the Indian partner) does manufacturing, field testing, partnerships with employers, and runs the business at a profit; MIT D-Lab focuses on technology design, content development, and learning-outcome measurement and analysis. JumpToPC will be a case study for analyzing the arguments used for decisions that move the project forward.

## Video 3 (04:30) — Deductive Reasoning

In this short segment we talk about deductive reasoning and about fallacies — gaps or faults in deductive reasoning. **Deduction** is a process of reasoning from premises to conclusions. Premises and conclusions are both **propositions** — linguistic expressions that can be either true or false. Premises build arguments that lead to conclusions. In deductive reasoning, if the reasoning is sound, we can proceed from true premises to true conclusions reliably. Conclusions drawn via deduction are not any broader than the premises allow — generally they say something less broad than the premises actually indicate. **Induction**, by contrast, is a process of going from particulars to more general statements (addressed in week two). Deductive reasoning is powerful because it guarantees that if all premises are true and the deductive reasoning is sound, the conclusions reached are necessarily true; it is agnostic about content.

When the process goes wrong we identify fallacies. A **syllogistic** argument relates to classes or categories. A **propositional** fallacy occurs when the structure connecting propositions with words like "and" or "or" goes awry. **Fallacies of quantification** involve words like all, none, some, few in premises and conclusions. The syllogistic, propositional, and quantification fallacies are in the realm of formal deductive logic, but there are also informal fallacies. An example of an informal fallacy is the **ad hominem** argument: if you drift away from addressing the premises or logical structure of an argument and instead attack the person making it, you commit ad hominem. (Assessing the reliability of people is not irrational — if someone lies all the time, maybe stop listening to them — but addressing only the source rather than the substance is, strictly speaking, the informal fallacy of ad hominem.) The coming videos focus on the formal deductive fallacies, starting with syllogistic fallacies.

## Video 4 (05:22) — Syllogism

Here is a classic example from the Greek classics: All men are mortal; Socrates is a man; therefore Socrates is mortal. This is a valid argument — the premises are true and the conclusion follows. "All men are mortal" is the **major premise**; "Socrates is a man" is the **minor premise**; "therefore Socrates is mortal" is the conclusion. It follows necessarily from the premises — the form of a valid argument called a syllogism.

We can construct similar syllogisms in realistic domains such as JumpToPC: All laptops have CPUs (true). The Asus X540 (a particular low-cost laptop model) has to have a CPU or it wouldn't be a laptop. Therefore the Asus X540 has a CPU. This is valid deductive logic.

Here is a syllogism that has gone wrong — a syllogistic fallacy. Major premise: all laptops have CPUs. Minor premise: no smartphone is a laptop (correct — smartphones are a distinct category). The conclusion someone tries to draw is: therefore no smartphone has a CPU — definitely not true; smartphones always have CPUs. The logic is wrong. This error has a name: **illicit major**, with the general form "all A are B; no C are A; therefore no C are B" — an incorrect construction in deductive logic regardless of content. A **Venn diagram** reveals the logical structure more clearly: category B (everything inside the circle is B); an oval A inside B (all A are B); an oval C disjoint from A (no C are A). It is not true that no C are B — there can be an overlap between C and B even though A is within B and C is disjoint from A. Meta-cognitively: if you're a project team leader and someone brings you an argument like this, you want to help them understand why their reasoning has gone awry — Venn diagrams can help others on the team strengthen their critical thinking skills.

## Video 5 (06:25) — Propositional Fallacies

Propositional logic is a logical system for deducing valid compound propositions from simpler propositions. If it's true that "P and (Q or R)," that implies "(P and Q) or (P and R)" — P distributes over "Q or R." Similarly, "not (P and Q)" implies "not P or not Q" — "not" distributes and transforms "and" into "or." These truths are reliable in deductive logic regardless of the content of P, Q, and R.

A form of propositional logic is **modus tollens**: If an intruder came into our home, the dog would have barked. The dog didn't bark. Therefore an intruder did not come into our home. This is valid (assuming a dog that always barks at strangers). Applied to the case study: If your device has a sufficiently large screen, a CPU, and a keyboard, then you can make effective use of a spreadsheet. If a particular smartphone doesn't enable effective use of a spreadsheet (verified by a community test), a valid inference is that this smartphone must lack either a sufficiently large screen, a CPU, or a keyboard. Since the smartphone already has a CPU, adding a large screen and keyboard would enable spreadsheet use.

Another valid form is **modus ponens**: If you have a laptop, it will promote your digital literacy. Kamala has a laptop. Therefore Kamala's digital literacy is being promoted. (In informal logic the laptop would have to be sufficient by itself, but assuming the premise is true, the conclusion follows.)

## Video 6 (03:23) — Quantification Fallacies

Quantification involves terms like all, some, most, few, or none. The general rule: when you use quantifiers in the premises, there should be agreement with the quantification in the conclusions — generally, you don't want to broaden as you go from premises to conclusions.

Example applied to JumpToPC: Most Indian households have an affordable data plan for their smartphone (rising quickly past 50%). Our target is to reach 20% of that population. If all Indian households with an affordable data plan adopted JumpToPC, we would meet our target — since 50% is greater than 20%, there's a comfortable margin.

An example of quantificational reasoning gone wrong (a slippery one): "All affordable laptop computers enable low-income Indian households to gain computer skills; therefore at least some laptop computer models would enable low-income Indian households to gain computer skills." This does not follow, because there aren't any affordable laptop computers in India today (a laptop costs more than a month's pay for the low-income communities addressed). In formal logic, "all affordable laptop computers enable this" is a true statement even though none exist — because if they did, it would be true. You can't conclude that "at least some" do, because they don't exist. This is the **existential fallacy**: in quantificational deductive reasoning you should not presuppose that a class has members when it's not valid to do so.

## Video 7 (06:37) — Thinking about Deductive Thinking

We explored the full factorial table of premises being true or false and deductive reasoning being sound or unsound. Now consider: if we know the truth value of the conclusion with certainty, and we know whether the reasoning is sound or unsound, which combinations let us make a conclusion with certainty? Only one: **when the deductive reasoning is sound and the conclusion is false, we can validly infer that at least one premise must be false** — because if all premises were true, the conclusion would have to be true. The other combinations are indeterminate.

Example from the case study: Premise — "Wi-Fi is necessary to remotely upload and download digital files such as spreadsheets" (I believe this false; wired internet in an office would work). Premise — "Upload and download of digital files is necessary for modern office work" (true). Provisional conclusion — "Wi-Fi must be available to remote workers in a modern office" (I believe false; in JumpToPC we're gambling cellular data plans will be sufficient). The logic's structure is valid — if the two premises were true they would lead necessarily to the conclusion — so false premises combined with sound logic lead necessarily to a false conclusion.

If conclusions and premises are both known true or false, only one combination is reliably diagnostic: if the conclusion is false and the premises are true, at least some part of the reasoning has gone awry — inspect your logical process and find the fault.

Case study example: "If a television is connected to an aerial antenna on the roof, then it is possible to broadcast to that television. It is possible to broadcast to a particular television. Therefore this television is connected to an aerial antenna." This is false (using mirror-cast to broadcast). The reasoning is **affirming the consequent** — fallacious. Another: "If Hindi is an official language of a state, the majority of people in that state can speak Hindi" (true). "The majority of people in Maharashtra do not speak Hindi" (true). "Hindi is not an official language of Maharashtra" (true). The form is modus tollens — sound reasoning. But switching particulars: "If Marathi is an official language of a state, the majority can speak Marathi. The majority of people in Maharashtra can speak Marathi. Therefore Marathi is an official language of Maharashtra." The conclusion is true, but the structure is affirming the consequent — it led to a true conclusion only because only one state has Marathi as an official language; it would not in general reliably lead to true conclusions.

## Video 9 (03:48) — Deductive Summary

This week focused on deductive reasoning, which allows us with certainty to go from premises to conclusions. Moving forward we'll address much more complex arguments: we'll need **induction** to generalize from particular facts to broader statements, and tools such as **argument diagrams** to show the structure of more complex arguments, building up a reasoned belief in our assertions even without perfect certainty.

Re-emphasizing the definition: critical thinking is a deliberate, meta-cognitive and cognitive act whereby a person reflects on the quality of the reasoning process even as they reason to a conclusion. We looked at the diagnostic abilities deductive reasoning gives us — finding faults in premises and mistakes in reasoning — and at the limits of deductive reasoning, knowing when it leaves a situation indeterminate.

An example from the case study that goes beyond deductive reasoning: India's population includes a large proportion of IT professionals. The language situation is complex — 31 languages are spoken by a million or more people, plus more languages and dialects. English is an important subsidiary official language of India next to Hindi; although most people don't speak English, a large number do. About two out of three women in India can read and write in some language. We provisionally draw the conclusion, for JumpToPC, that a significant proportion of women in India will be able to learn a graphical user interface written in English — JumpToPC doesn't have enough capacity to make all the translations. To make a conjecture like that, critical to a project, you construct arguments based on evidence and build a reasoned belief before pressing forward. Next week we develop our capacity to work with tools like argument diagrams and Bayes' Law for inductive inference.
