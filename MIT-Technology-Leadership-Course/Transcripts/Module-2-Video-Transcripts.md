## Video 1 — Case Study (06:27) — Unintended Acceleration at Toyota

This week focuses on tools for more complex analysis of arguments, beginning with a second case study: the Toyota unintended acceleration events prominent in the media in the early 2000s. In these incidents, a driver reports the engine RPMs increase even though they haven't pressed the gas pedal, and the car accelerates as long as the transmission is engaged — often the driver also reports the brakes were ineffective. Both Toyota and Lexus experienced a large number of these events; the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration estimates about 16,000 unintended acceleration accidents are reported each year.

These events could be caused by several things. Very common is driver error — the driver thinks they're pressing the brake but is pressing the accelerator. There can also be mechanical failures, and in modern cars electronic or software faults — a focus of the case study. From 2002 to 2009 there was an unusual volume of customer reports on Toyota and Lexus vehicles. In March 2007 Toyota first determined that some floor mats were engaged with the pedal, causing "entrapment" (the pedal pressed down and held by the floor mat). From September 2009 to March 2010 Toyota conducted seven recalls affecting about 10 million vehicles — for floor mats and for purely mechanical "sticky pedals" — but ultimately no recalls related to electronic sensors or software.

The NTSB (National Transportation Safety Board) conducted an investigation and wrote a report "Technical Assessment of Toyota Electronic Throttle Control Systems," and commissioned NASA for a separate parallel report focused on engineering design. The first study analyzed tens of thousands of Toyota documents, considering causes from electromagnetic interference (the most exotic idea) to routine driver error, and found floor mats and sticky pedals were clearly the cause of certain incidents. The NASA study laid out the system architecture: on a car with an electronic throttle control system there is still a pedal, but it is not connected by mechanical linkage to the throttle — instead two sensors report to the engine control module, which also takes in airflow and other vehicle sensors (like accelerometers), and sends out signals to open the throttle and control fuel injection and ignition. The throttle (air), fuel injection (fuel), and ignition coil (spark) together create combustion and acceleration.

An additional report came from an organization called The Safety Record, whose mission is to improve public safety and which engages in civil litigation and lobbying for regulatory changes. Their report focused on scenarios where engine speed could increase and the throttle open in contradiction to the driver's command — events that were truly unintended and did not set a diagnostic trouble code (so the recordings normally made by the engine control system might not have been made). They focused on a NASA finding about "tin whiskers" generated under the hood that found their way into the pedal position sensors, and cited a report that owners of Camrys with the new electronic systems reported acceleration at greater rates than corresponding manual throttle systems before any news coverage. They also claimed Toyota was too heavily involved in guiding the research behind the NTSB and NASA reports, and criticized the NTSB for lacking a regulatory baseline for safety-critical electronics and for a lack of independence. There is plenty of controversy and grist for discussion about critical thinking and decision making.

## Video 2 (06:02) — Basic Argument Diagrams

This video introduces **argument diagrams** — a tool that analyzes the structure of critical thinking so we can better understand an argument you or someone else is making. The benefits: it forces you to study an argument in detail (the human mind works best when it can focus); it reveals the logical structure by laying out the basic components and showing graphical cues to interconnections — it reveals the architecture of an argument; and visualization recruits outstanding human intellectual capacities.

Scientists often describe the human brain as three brains in one: the **reptilian complex** (lower/central, associated with digestion, circulation, the fight-or-flight reflex); the **limbic system** or "mammalian brain" (emotional responses like love or anger, and importantly long-term memory); and the **neocortex** (the newest part — sensory processing, motor control, and general intelligence such as language, logic, and mathematics). About one-third of the neocortex is the **visual cortex**, connected to the retina through about a million nerve fibers. The visual cortex is involved in much thinking that is not strictly about vision — for instance, mentally rotating an object with your eyes closed. Making a diagram of an argument recruits the visual cortex to help you understand it ("I get the picture").

An argument from the case study: conclusion — most of the reported unintended acceleration events were due to driver error. Supporting facts from the NHTSA report: (1) parking space entry and exit accounted for 64% of the crashes (why would an electronic problem happen mostly when pulling out of a parking space?); (2) a disproportionate number of incidents happened with drivers over age 60 (an electronic cause wouldn't favor older drivers); (3) in a substantial proportion of cases, electronic data recorders showed no evidence of attempts to brake. All three premises lend independent support — if one were not true, the others still apply equally strongly. This is the simplest kind of argument diagram.

## Video 3 (03:48) — Arguments with Sub-Conclusions

An argument from the Toyota case: The electronic throttle control system in the Camry was designed so that high acceleration would only occur if the gas pedal was depressed more than 50%. The sensing system is redundant with two sensors reporting to the engine control computer; if their values do not match, no more than 18 degrees of throttle are ever applied. The braking system can generate 0.25g or more at up to 35 degrees of throttle opening. The electronic throttle control system had a relatively smooth launch — after introduction, the rate of unintended-acceleration complaints stayed mostly constant and even dropped (though not statistically significant). Therefore the electronic throttle control system is unlikely to be the cause of the Toyota unintended acceleration problems.

Breaking it into pieces: the **conclusion** (the electronic throttle control system was not the cause) appears at the end, as conclusions often do. It is supported by three independent premises: a compelling reason to suspect driver error; no increase in unintended-acceleration reports with the new system; and the engineering design itself was configured to avoid such accidents. That third premise is itself supported by two **sub-premises**: two redundant sensors measure pedal position (redundancy reduces the likelihood the sensors are at fault); and the braking system can generate one-quarter g of braking even with the throttle 35 degrees open. Those two independently support the **sub-conclusion** that the engineering design was effective, which combines with the other two premises to support the final conclusion. This is an argument within an argument.

## Video 4 (04:23) — Arguments with Linked Premises

The previous video showed sub-conclusions where premises were relatively independent. Now, **linked premises**. A verbal argument relevant to the case study (constructed for instruction — not the exact NTSB/NASA findings): A concern has been raised about possible failures of pedal sensors due to tin whiskers, but this could not be the cause of the UA events. If two pedal sensors give different indications, an event is logged by the engine computer. None of the vehicles involved in UA accidents had a logged event of that type. This means the pedal sensors were giving the same indications for all the vehicles in the UA reports. The only way this is consistent with pedal sensor failures is for two sensors to fail simultaneously and create identical un-commanded acceleration indications. When two pedal sensors fail, they are unlikely to give the same indications. Therefore none of the reported UA incidents involving Toyotas were caused by pedal position sensor failures.

Diagramming it: the conclusion is supported by a chain. "If two pedal sensors give different indications, an event is logged" requires the **linked** premise "none of the reported crashes had sensor failure events logged" — together they support the sub-conclusion that the pedal sensors were giving the same indications. They are linked because if the logging system were unreliable, the absence of logged events would no longer be strong evidence. That sub-conclusion links to a further premise: when two pedal sensors fail, they are unlikely to give the same indications. With three independent premises all linked with a "plus," the diagram reveals an important architectural fact: this argument is **brittle** — a convincing case against any one of the three premises makes the whole argument fall.

## Video 5 (05:49) — Arguments with Unstated Premises

Constructing argument diagrams that fill in **unstated premises** — things essential to the soundness of an argument that the arguer didn't explicitly articulate — is an important skill. A verbal argument from the case study (representing people defending the interests of injured parties): An electronic throttle may look and feel like the conventional mechanical arrangement, but it is a radically different system. Electronic and software-based automotive systems have far more components and interconnections than the mechanical systems they replace. Due to system complexity and the many possible hardware/software interactions, it is not realistic to prove that the electronic throttle system cannot cause unintended acceleration. Even if the probability of a UA event in an individual vehicle is low, within a fleet of millions such events may affect thousands of vehicles. We are compelled to conclude that electronic controls of safety-critical automotive systems represent an increased risk to the public and are worthy of greater attention.

The three stated premises: (1) the electronic throttle looks/feels like the mechanical system but is not the same; (2) system complexity and complex software interactions; (3) low individual probabilities applied to many instances accumulate to a large number of cases. Added **unstated premises**: (A) mechanical throttles are safe enough — otherwise why argue about the system being replaced; (B) the surface similarity of the new electronic system does not imply a deeper similarity, so consumers might be fooled; (C) proof of safety has to be completed within constraints (true — even very large computers cannot prove that a moderately complex system can never enter a particular state); (D) incidents affecting thousands of vehicles deserve the attention of safety regulators (but what is the threshold? — shouldn't regulators worry more about tens or hundreds of thousands?). Identifying and articulating unstated premises gives you an opportunity to attack someone else's argument or to strengthen your own by supporting your unstated premises with data.

## Video 6 (05:27) — Arguments with Counterpoints

Diagramming an argument that contains **counterpoints** — a common rhetorical strategy where you state your argument and within it articulate a counterargument against yourself, anticipating objections and thereby strengthening your case. A verbal argument from the Toyota case, from the perspective of someone within Toyota: One possible solution to the unintended acceleration concerns is to use brake pedal deflection to override gas pedal commands. This would have prevented some of the pedal entrapment accidents. Consumers have expressed stress over the coverage of UA events. The idea of brake pedal override was tested with focus groups and perceived positively. Other companies are considering a similar change. Some states have expressed concern that their annual safety inspections involve application of brakes and throttle — but those tests could be conducted using the parking brakes instead. We have to seriously consider the brake override option.

Diagram: three premises independently support the conclusion (the design of the engine control system should be changed so that braking automatically brings the engine to idle): (1) the change would be responsive to consumers' stated concerns; (2) there are scenarios it would have averted — including a highly publicized case of an off-duty California highway patrolman killed while reportedly pressing the brakes as hard as he could; (3) a competitor is discussing a similar change. A **counterargument** is added (in a different font/color): braking effectiveness at rest could not be tested using the engine — a concern for state inspectors. The **counter to the counter** — the parking brake could be used for that safety test — is marked with a minus sign showing it goes against the counterargument. The graphical visualization helps show how the pieces fit into a coherent whole.

## Video 7 (03:24) — Bayes' Law: Introduction

Moving to quantify support for an argument using **Bayes' law**. The symbolic form: the probability of A given B (the vertical line means B is given/known true) equals the probability of A times the probability of B given A, divided by the probability of B. In a Venn diagram, events A and B overlap (A ∩ B), surrounded by the universal event U (everything that could possibly happen). We divide by the probability of B because, given that B is true, everything that is not B is eliminated and we re-normalize the sample space — leaving B and A ∩ B; A ∩ B is the numerator and the probability of B the denominator.

Applied to hypotheses and data: replace A with H (hypothesis) and B with D (data) — the probability of H given D. The more complicated part is calculating the probability of D — imagining how likely the data was even before you saw it, by summing the probability of the data under all the different hypotheses.

## Video 8 (04:47) — Bayes' Law: Application to the Toyota Case

An example from the case study, from the perspective of a consumer with a Toyota Camry who learns there are tin whiskers in their pedal sensors, wondering whether to take a maintenance action. Relevant facts: tin whiskers were present in 90% of the pedal sensors of automobiles that had reported UA events; whiskers are found in one in three pedal sensors on random inspections (cars that mostly didn't have UA events); only one in 2,000 vehicles experiences unintended acceleration in a year.

To find the probability of unintended acceleration given the tin whiskers: the base rate of unintended acceleration in the population (given as 5,000 vehicles out of a fleet of 10 million) is multiplied by the probability of tin whiskers given unintended acceleration (the "power of the test" — how often crashed cars have tin whiskers). The denominator is the probability of tin whiskers under both scenarios — the numerator term plus a second term using the false-alarm rate of one-third multiplied by the probability of no unintended acceleration. Running the calculation, the probability of an unintended acceleration event given the tin whiskers is still only about **0.135%**. Even though the mechanic found tin whiskers, a UA event is still very unlikely; removing the whiskers or buying new pedal sensors may not be worth it, especially since you now have a way of dealing with it (e.g., shifting the car into neutral). Bayes' law helps you decide when to take an action and commit resources based on quantitative facts. Its one problem is that it is complicated — people often don't feel they understand the result — which motivates the next tool, frequency formats.

## Video 9 (05:11) — Frequency Formats

**Frequency formats** help people better understand the support for an argument based on quantitative data. The same Camry/tin-whiskers decision: 90% of automobiles that had UA events had tin whiskers; about a third of all vehicles on the road have tin whiskers even without UA events; only one in 2,000 vehicles experiences the event in a year.

There is empirical evidence that people have a hard time understanding Bayes' law. Psychologist Gerd Gigerenzer showed doctors a probabilistic (Bayes'-law) formulation of a scenario involving a positive mammogram test and the probability of a patient having a disease — most doctors gave the wrong answer, thinking a single positive mammogram meant a very high likelihood of breast cancer. When Gigerenzer restated the same information in a **frequency format**, the doctors overwhelmingly chose the correct answer — only about 9% of women with a single positive mammogram actually have the disease.

Applied to the case study: pick a population and describe it with a realistic number — 10 million Camrys (roughly the number on the road). Build a tree branching down: Camrys that never had a UA event (the majority — 9.5 million) and the 5,000 UA events (the base rate). Subdivide further: of those that never had a UA event, about a third (3.17 million) had tin whiskers; of those that had a UA event, the vast majority (90% — 4,500) had tin whiskers. You know you are in one of two groups — 4,500 with a UA event and tin whiskers, or 3.17 million with tin whiskers but no UA event. The probability you are in the UA group is 4,500 ÷ (3,170,000 + 4,500) ≈ **0.13%**. Having constructed this diagram and understood why the base rate and false-alarm rate matter, you can confidently decide not to replace the pedal sensor — statistically you are still very unlikely to have the event. Frequency formats support a gut-level understanding of the implications of Bayes' law for your scenario.
