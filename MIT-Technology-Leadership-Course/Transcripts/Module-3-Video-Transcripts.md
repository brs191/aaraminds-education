## Video 1 (05:00) — Introduction to Group Decision Making

We move into decision making in general, and specifically decision making in groups. Earlier the course focused on deduction and reasoning from premises to conclusions, then on more complex reasoning (argument diagrams) and quantitative methods for inductive reasoning. Now we pivot to decision making, where we have alternatives and real consequences. **Decision making is a process of choosing among alternatives based on values, preferences, and beliefs.** Commitment to a decision involves an irrevocable allocation of resources. If you have only bad alternatives, making a decision leads to bad outcomes — so decision making should also involve creating better alternatives, synthesizing new ones from your analysis.

Professor Thomas Saaty captured the central themes: decision making should interest every living person; the complexity of our world requires considering multiple perspectives; conflicts of interest arise and decision-making processes help resolve them; complexity forces these considerations upon us. Because we deal with complex issues, no one person has all the information needed — we must work with groups to get the best results.

As a visiting professor at TU Delft in the Netherlands, the instructor investigated the **Polder Model** of decision making — characterized by lengthy deliberations and a focus on building consensus before deciding. A *polder* is land reclaimed from the sea, below sea level, kept dry by dikes and pumps (often windmill-driven). People in the Netherlands have maintained dikes and windmills since the Middle Ages — even when two cities in the same polder were at war, they continued maintaining the shared infrastructure, because otherwise all would suffer. This created a mindset of cooperation, consensus building, and lasting commitment to decisions.

## Video 2 (08:25) — Pugh Decision Matrix Basics

The main tool is **Pugh Controlled Convergence** — a method for making decisions involving all participants and laying out all considerations so they can be visualized clearly. **Stuart Pugh** had a degree in Mechanical Engineering and long experience in private industry making big decisions; near the end of his career he focused on teaching and developing methodologies like controlled convergence.

The Pugh method relies on a **matrix** — a complex but two-dimensional representation of the facts. Columns represent the alternatives (e.g., design concepts); rows describe the criteria for the decision. Inside the body of the matrix are evaluations of how well each alternative performs against each criterion.

Choosing criteria: the attributes that matter to customers and stakeholders should be prominent — this is an opportunity to face your organization toward what's most important and pivot toward an external emphasis. Because this is a group process, criteria should be ones people will understand (or can understand once explained). Criteria should clearly discriminate among alternatives — if every alternative is roughly the same against a criterion, leave it out. Keep the list moderate (around seven to thirteen); with 11 alternatives and 13 criteria you'd need 130 separate conversations.

When filling out the matrix, use a **datum concept** — choose one alternative and make all comparisons relative to it (write "datum" into that column). This makes the information and conversations grounded and concrete. A concern (per Nobel-winning economist Kenneth Arrow's "independence of irrelevant alternatives") is that this introduces an arbitrary element — but if you choose a well-understood leading alternative as the datum (e.g., a Honda Fit when evaluating economy cars), your decision making becomes much clearer.

It is important to have a **facilitator** managing the process — controlling the flow and pace, putting marks on the board, maintaining discipline, bringing people back to comparing against the datum, clarifying criteria, and encouraging the generation of new alternatives.

The method helps overcome challenges in group decision making: experienced, impatient people may want to rush, but less-experienced people may have influential things to say; a person who feels strongly about an alternative may become defensive ("self-sealing" behavior), and a little structure helps them be patient and listen. The Pugh method is good for structuring the decision process, encouraging convergence, and facilitating creativity — but it is **not** meant for automating decisions (it is a guide only) and is not suited to detailed trade studies. It is best for relatively early design stages where new alternatives can still be created.

## Video 3 (04:10) — Case Study: MIT D-Lab

(Kendra Leith) D-Lab works with people worldwide to develop collaborative approaches and practical solutions to global poverty challenges. Its work includes educating MIT undergraduates and graduate students, an innovation-practice arm focused on fieldwork, and a research arm (which Kendra leads). The research arm conducts studies on needs assessment, asset mapping, understanding local context, technology evaluation and development, and local innovation — across energy, agriculture, water, local innovation systems, and mobile (especially mobile health) technologies. D-Lab's participatory design approach designs not only *for* people but *with* them, building their capacity to design for themselves.

One example is **evaporative cooling**: a simple technology placing one pot inside another with sand and water in between — as the water evaporates it creates a humid, cool environment for fruits and vegetables, critical where people lack refrigeration or electricity. D-Lab comparatively evaluated different evaporative cooling devices in the lab and field (humidity, temperature, moisture), obtained follow-on funding to iterate and test designs (pot technologies and larger brick-based systems), modeled and evaluated outcomes, and is now training others to construct and sell the devices — extending the shelf life of produce, reducing market trips, and increasing farmer incomes. D-Lab also studies innovation systems and how they change, and maps local innovation ecosystems (stakeholders, interactions, existing assets, gaps).

## Video 4 (03:41) — How MIT D-Lab Uses the Pugh Matrix

(Kendra Leith) The Pugh Matrix evaluates a set of concepts against a set of criteria, compared to a particularly strong concept (the datum). It lets you take a deep dive into important topics and evaluate them to select one. D-Lab uses Pugh to prioritize research questions and areas, and to select and decide which projects to fund. It lets the group discuss the criteria and what's important (e.g., impact potential or need); it identifies where new information is needed when there is disagreement or uncertainty; and it creates buy-in because decisions are made together, collaboratively and transparently, rather than opaquely by one person.

Decision making is hard because it is not always clear what is important to people or why — people go with their gut without knowing why; many interests are subconscious. Power dynamics also make it hard, leaving some people feeling they don't have a voice. Making a decision cuts off other options, which people resist. Pugh helps address these: it clarifies criteria ahead of time, lets people articulate their issues in a structured way, and helps explain where gut feelings come from — breaking critical strategic decisions down into digestible, transparent, thoughtful parts.

## Video 5 (03:12) — Analyzing Group Decisions

(Guest practitioner) What if a group seems to be going in an unproductive or incorrect direction? First ask: why do I disagree? If it's only because it wasn't my idea, that's not a good reason to shake the tree. If there is an actual technical or procedural reason that the direction is inappropriate or dangerous, bring it up. Consider: if they go that way, what's the worst that can happen? If "not much," let them go and save yourself the heartache and bad capital. If it matters and can't be stepped back from — risk to life, limb, property, careers — you have an obligation to raise it.

Egos are often in play. It can help to say, "This is a great group — I just don't understand how we came to this conclusion. Can you help me understand?" Tease out unsupported opinions and claims with no hard evidence, rather than a full-frontal assault — a cognitive assault can trigger the **backfire effect**, where people double down on a bad idea (the same fight/flight/freeze response as a physical assault). Take a softer approach and lead them through a thought process. Sometimes they'll solve it themselves; other times you can't, and you must decide whether to stay with the group and mitigate the damage or bail out. Conclusions and decisions are never perfect, but a consistent and robust approach to examining evidence and making decisions is the best you can do.

## Video 6 (05:28) — Pairwise Comparison

Once you choose the datum concept, you always compare one alternative to that datum — a **paired comparison**. In the JumpToPC case, when considering antennas (a microstrip antenna, an LDS antenna, and a UWB planar aperture antenna) evaluated on latency, data rate, and reliability, the microstrip antenna was the datum. A plus under "data rate" for the LDS antenna means its data rate is better than the microstrip's; a minus under "data rate" for the UWB antenna means it is less good than the datum — not in some absolute sense.

This feature reflects the **law of comparative judgment**. An **absolute judgment** determines a quantity's value relative to a scale (e.g., "What is the temperature today?"). A **relative judgment** (e.g., "Is today colder or warmer than yesterday?") is generally much easier and more accurate — humans are very good at relative judgments. If you make all the pairwise comparisons needed, you can produce an accurate, complete ordering of a set of alternatives. A psychology study asking people to judge line lengths showed that "discrimination" judgments transmit about twice as many bits of information as "magnitude estimation" tasks, with less variability and greater accuracy. Recommendation: choose a datum so you can make pairwise comparisons; pick one of the better, well-understood, familiar alternatives; and don't get bogged down choosing it — even a coin flip would give good results, though a strong datum works better.

## Video 7 (06:09) — Convergence: Dominance

Once the Pugh Matrix is filled, you must make decisions — eliminating some alternatives to attain **convergence** so you stop discussing poor choices. People often summarize the matrix (e.g., counting pluses). One approach creates a score by subtracting the number of minuses from the number of pluses — reasonable, but you should not ultimately decide on that basis. A better criterion that doesn't depend on counting is **dominance**: an alternative is **dominated** if some other option is either better or the same in **every** respect. A dominated alternative can be eliminated — keeping the dominating concept retains all the good things and loses none.

If a column has no pluses at all, that concept is dominated by the datum (easy to spot). Comparing two non-datum concepts is trickier: comparing concept 4 and concept 8 criterion by criterion, concept 8 is the same or better than concept 4 on every criterion — so 8 dominates 4, and 4 can be eliminated. Research with colleagues in the Netherlands showed the convergence attainable in a single Pugh-matrix run: in one case study, alternatives went from about 30 down to about 15 — roughly half eliminated through dominance. You don't always get that much, but it is not unusual.

## Video 8 (06:13) — Convergence: Conformity

Group dynamics in deciding to eliminate an option can run into trouble. The famous **Asch conformity experiment**: subjects at a table judge which of three lines matches a reference line — an easy judgment. But most of the people in the room were **confederates** instructed to give a clearly wrong answer ("A was longer"). After hearing the wrong judgment repeated, a substantial proportion of real subjects went along with the group. Some knew the right answer but didn't want to confront others; in other cases their perception itself seemed affected. Nothing was at stake — imagine how much more they would conform if the others had authority.

A real case: the **Pontiac Aztek**, meant to combine SUV, sedan, and minivan features (with quirky features like the ability to camp in the back, a strange trim line and lighting). It was one of the first cars shaped primarily through CAD and 3D printing rather than clay sculpting. The car looked gruesome and sold poorly — a little over 27,000 units, about 50% of those to a captive audience (GM executives, rental fleets). A GM product boss was quoted: "We'd fire the guy who greenlighted the Aztek, if we could find anyone willing to admit it." One wonders why people in those meetings kept going forward when someone should have raised concerns about the aesthetics.

As a counterpoint: **Herman Miller**, a well-regarded office furniture maker, developed a new chair with a mesh back, wing-like armrests, and new adjustability. Market research found people thought it comfortable and good for their back but could not see buying it because of its appearance. Some project leaders decided to go against the market research — and the chair became one of the most successful products in Herman Miller's history. So when a person of long experience or intuition decides to go against data, it is often a good thing. The course does not want you to follow the process religiously or always undermine an individual's view with the majority's — take a balanced perspective and recognize that experience and intuition are often an important positive force in group decision making.

## Video 9 (05:30) — Gathering More Information

The quality of a decision can never be better than the quality of the information it's based on. Don't feel so rushed that you assume more information can't be gathered. When building Pugh Matrices, an **S** symbol is sometimes inserted when performance is substantially the same, or there isn't enough information, or there is disagreement — and between Pugh runs you can turn an S into a plus or minus with some homework.

Ways to gather more information: **search the literature** (reap the benefits of others' prior investigation); **inquire within your network** of experts. Those two should always be first, but often you must generate the information yourself: **develop a computer model** (e.g., MATLAB simulations for heat transfer, electronics performance, reliability); **run an experiment** (a lot of authority backs experimental information — be frugal, a simple experiment often suffices); **build prototypes** (with a range of styles and investment levels). In the JumpToPC case, the team considered a customized trackball — a 3D model with renderings could evaluate aesthetics with the target market; 3D printing (e.g., Zcorp printers invented at MIT, which bind layers of powder using inkjet technology) could yield high-resolution prototypes for ergonomics and function; and more complex, expensive prototypes nearly identical to the final component could resolve technical questions like electronics packaging. Very simple, low-cost, quick prototypes are strongly recommended when they suffice to answer the question.

## Video 10 (03:19) — Enlightened Experimentation

Stefan Thomke's *Harvard Business Review* article "Enlightened Experimentation" makes four points. (1) **Organize for learning** — at BMW, sophisticated crash-testing simulations were rarely shared until the builders had high confidence, holding information so long that high-impact decisions were already made. Don't hold onto information. (2) **Fail early and fail often to succeed sooner** — at IDEO, the corporate culture did not stigmatize failure but celebrated it, enabling faster information gathering. (3) **Use technology to provide information early** — at Chrysler, struggling with the layout of brake lines and fuel lines, technologies like 3D printing and digital mock-ups accelerated decisions that didn't need reversing later. (4) **Combine new and traditional technologies** — at Eli Lilly, new technologies like combinatorial chemistry accelerated early information generation, but the firm reverted to traditional wet-lab chemistry to reach the final technology readiness level; the key is knowing when to switch.

## Video 11 (06:12) — Experts and Intuition

Expertise plays a role in decision making, and **intuition** is a way long experience and knowledge can make decision making appear effortless (as in the Herman Miller chair decision). Two research programs relate to experts making decisions. The **Heuristics and Biases** program focuses on deviations of humans from reasonable prescriptions about how people should decide (e.g., Bayes' law) — a somewhat negative view, noting human mistakes. The **Naturalistic Decision Making** program focuses on positive attributes — the success of experts in real environments.

A famous Heuristics-and-Biases result is **base rate neglect** (doctors thinking a cancer probability was 90% when it was about nine percent) — identified and correctable through proper application of Bayes' law. Naturalistic Decision Making studied firefighters who make remarkable leaps of intuition (pulling people out moments before a building collapses).

Distinctions between the programs: Heuristics and Biases works mostly in a controlled lab; Naturalistic Decision Making works in the actual field. Heuristics and Biases judges decisions by **coherence** (adherence to a process dictum); Naturalistic Decision Making judges by **outcome** or frequency of outcomes — one focused on process, one on outcome. Heuristics and Biases concerns **System 2** operations (slow, weighing data, calculations); Naturalistic Decision Making focuses on **System 1** operations (fast judgments based on memory and pattern recognition).

Despite differences, both programs agree that expert intuition exists — some people consistently make very good decisions — and that intuition is not mystical but based on memory and pattern recognition, and so understandable through science, with limits. Two conditions: the decision environment must be sufficiently **valid** (stable patterns a person can learn and exploit), and there must be adequate **opportunities to practice** (making decisions, seeing outcomes). Both also agree experts should not work only alone — in some areas you must rely on algorithms; if you can gather enough data and apply enough computation, don't let people work unassisted.

## Video 12 (02:14) — Pre-Mortem Analysis

The **pre-mortem analysis** is an effective emerging group technique. Bring together a team that has made a decision and formed a plan; develop a scenario in which the plan goes wrong; rely on people to develop **prospective hindsight** — criticizing the decision, the process, and the outcome by imagining something has actually gone wrong, looking back to see why and what could have been done differently. This reduces a systematic bias toward overconfidence and often improves the final decision. When developing designs, programs, or processes, people want to be positive; the pre-mortem gives cognitive permission to think negatively — to imagine openly everything that could go wrong, then rank and prioritize which are most devastating and which can be guarded against. A diverse group helps, because catastrophe looks different to the finance person, the manufacturer, the engineer, and the analyst.

## Video 13 (05:33) — Divergence and Decisions

**Divergence** makes a big difference in practical decision making. Pugh Controlled Convergence emphasizes going from a larger set of alternatives down to a smaller set, but it is important to also sometimes increase the range of alternatives. In Stuart Pugh's conceptualization, the vertical axis is time/progression; periods where the two lines move together are **convergence** (a Pugh matrix eliminating weaker alternatives), but there should also be periods where the funnel widens because new alternatives have been generated.

Treat the first Pugh-matrix run as an initial convergence opportunity that can inspire divergence — a concept strong in most ways but with a single weakness can prompt you to invent solutions that reverse that weakness, or to create **hybrids** by combining a strong alternative with a complementary (otherwise weak) one whose strengths cover the strong concept's weaknesses. In a next run, expand the evaluation criteria and pick a **different datum** (reducing dependence on the arbitrary datum choice). Experience shows three or four runs with periods of divergence in between is often optimal. Netherlands research showed: starting with 15 concepts, simulating divergence and hybrid generation brought candidates down to about five (similar to a real engineering group); without divergence and hybrids, convergence only reached about 10. **Convergent and divergent thinking are like the two blades of a scissor — together they do something neither can do as effectively alone.**

## Video 14 (02:51) — The KNOT Chart

Whenever we feel we've found the answer, it is beneficial to ask: do we really know — what would it take to change my mind? We operate on **mental models**, which (like any model) are flawed, incomplete, and sometimes wrong. Asking "What would it take to change my mind?" acknowledges this and triggers an examination of assumptions.

The **KNOT chart**: **K — What do I Know?** (you can comfortably say you know something if you can provide supporting evidence). **N — What do I Need to know?** (outstanding questions). **O — Opinion** (what's my opinion). **T — What I Think I know** (distinguished from "Know" by whether there is a definitive reference — a document, history, or evidence — versus tribal knowledge, legend, or myth). Filling out the KNOT chart reveals what is fact- and data-based and what is not; anything not fact-based isn't necessarily wrong but should be verified. Balance all of this with "what happens if I'm wrong?" — if the answer is "not much, and it's immediately fixable," maybe just do it and see what happens (the Toyota Production System / Nike "just do it" philosophy) — just don't do anything you can't take back.
