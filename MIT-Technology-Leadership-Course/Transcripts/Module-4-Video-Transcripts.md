## Video 1 (05:02) — What Is System Thinking?

I'm Ed Crawley, a Professor of Aeronautics and Astronautics at MIT — a systems guy. You can tell a lot about a person by asking what systems they think about. My list: MIT as an organization, my family, the products I work on, the courses I teach, the nation I live in, and the planet. Systems we should all think about include the natural environment, national security, education, and health care — grand challenges that all require system thinking.

**System thinking is simply thinking of things as systems.** Systems are things that have entities and relationships, elements and connections. It has been important for decades, and even more so as the systems we rely on have become more complex and interrelated and the problems society faces more intractable. System thinking is essential to understanding the earth and its environment as a coupled system of biological, technical, and human elements, and is important beyond technical fields — in economics, politics, and social science.

One favorite example: Is this an elevator or a linear accelerator? The answer is yes to both — elevators once ran on simple cables and levers, but now have sophisticated control and backup emergency systems. Even simple devices become more complex because we ask more of them: more functionality, performance, reliability. A complex system has many parts and connections; with our limited cognitive ability it is too complicated. **System thinking works to make complex systems appear simple.** Sometimes you can't solve a task within the available scope — you're in a "stovepipe." System thinkers recognize this and expand the scope of the problem until they find a solution, letting them be creators and problem solvers in complex settings.

System thinking is different from thinking *systematically*. **A system is a set of interrelated entities that perform a function whose functionality is greater than the sum of the parts.** Almost every word matters: the system must have **entities** (modules, elements, chunks that are distinct and otherwise independent); these entities must be **interrelated**; the interrelated entities perform a **function** (what the system does); and this functionality is greater than the sum of the parts — something new or unexpected **emerges**. This is the magic of systems — **emergence**. Applying this to the elevator: the main entities are the car, the cable drive, and the shaft (the shaft constrains the car, the cable moves it); the emergence is the magic of stepping into an elevator on one floor and getting off on another. Become a system thinker because we live among complex systems, we need to make complex things feel simple, we need to be problem solvers in complex settings, and most importantly we need to understand emergence in systems.

## Video 2 (04:51) — Emergence

System thinkers view everything as a system. Four main ideas: there are entities; the entities are interrelated; the entities and relationships enable a function; the function is more powerful than the sum of the functions of the parts — **emergence**, providing both the magical power and the challenge of dealing with systems.

Examples: two entities — sand and a funnel — neither having anything to do with time, placed in a relationship become an **hourglass**, and a new function emerges: keeping time (the most accurate timekeeping device for thousands of years). **Function** is the most powerful type of emergence — run machine learning code and you get image recognition; get on an elevator and you're transported.

The 2018 French World Cup soccer team is a system of entities (the people) and relationships; its function, like any soccer team, is to score more goals than the opponent — but it had better **performance** than any other team in the world. **Performance** is an emergent property — transporting people in 30 seconds, recognizing an image of so many pixels in one second.

Before the Toyota Quality System of the 1980s, broken-down cars were a more common sight. A breakdown is a loss of **reliability**; fixing it relates to **serviceability**. Reliability and serviceability are the emergence of **non-functional attributes** — since many end in "ility," the **ilities** of the system.

Hurricane Katrina passing over the city of New Orleans — a new relationship dropping inches of rain — produced severe flooding: an **emergency**, which is just emergence that is destructive or undesirable (plagues, droughts; human-created building fires, explosions, crashes).

The **four types of emergence**: emergence of **function** (the system does something never done before); emergence of **performance** (the system does something better than before); emergence of the **ilities** / non-functional attributes; and the emergence of an **emergency** that we don't want to happen.

## Video 3 (04:08) — Function

The first task of system thinking is to identify the system, its form, and its function. **Function is what a system does** — the activities, operations, and transformations that cause or contribute to performance. Function is important because the magic of emergence occurs in the functional domain.

Examples: the function of the human circulatory system is to deliver oxygen to cells; the function of a work of art is to intrigue the viewer; the function of a debit card is to post the debit electronically. A function's description contains two parts: the change itself (the **process**) and the thing changed or operated on (the **operand**). The operand is a thing that exists whose states or attributes can be changed — it can be physical (the art viewer) or informational (a record of a debit). A process is a pattern of transformation that creates, changes, or destroys the operand; you cannot hold or touch a process — it is fleeting along a timeline (you need a movie, not a still picture).

More examples: a legged robot positioning a payload (process = moving, operand = the payload); an industrial supply chain (process = supplying, operand = goods); a product development team, "team X" (process = developing, operand = the new product). Interestingly, the builder of the system often doesn't supply the operand — the operand appears at the time of operation (the artist has no control over who views the art; the supply-chain distributor doesn't make the goods).

System function is related to the **benefit** delivered — focus on function to ensure the system delivers value. Every system has a **primary function** (the necessary thing it does, usually clear from designer intent or observation) and can acquire **secondary functions** (team X can send a report to management, but its primary function is developing the product). In summary, function is what a system does, how it creates benefit; it consists of an operand that is changed and a process that brings about the change; and emergence occurs in the functional domain.

## Video 4 (04:49) — Form

**Form is what the system is** — the physical or informational embodiment, involving material, configuration, text, and instructions. If uncertain whether something is form, ask: "Was it implemented — written, composed, manufactured, or created?" If so, it is probably form. Unlike "function," form has many names: mechanisms, molecules, facilities, circuits, building blocks, and infrastructure are physical form; poetry, literature, code, and instructions are informational form; people are part of the form of an organization. **Form is the instrument of function** and must be present for the function to be delivered.

Examples: delivering oxygen is the function, the circulatory system is the form; intriguing the viewer is the function, the piece of art is the form; posting a debit is the function, the card is the form. Form can be captured in an image; function requires a movie.

A simple **icon** for a system: a white box (function — operand and process) within a shaded box (named for the form) — reminding us that form and function are separate, complementary aspects.

**Value is benefit at cost.** Benefit is closely associated with function; cost is largely identified with form. For the legged robot, the benefit is largely associated with moving the payload, but the cost is largely associated with the parts and labor to make the form. For team X, the benefit is a successful product; the cost is incurred when the team is paid; good value is a quality product under budget.

**Abstractions** make complicated systems appear simple — like a wrapper around the system that conceals unneeded information and leaves on the surface only what is relevant (form, function, and if necessary performance and the ilities). The abstraction "legged robot" might show how many legs it has while concealing dimensions, articulation, software code, and sensors — and carry function/performance information (how quickly it can move, how much payload it can carry, how precisely it can position). In summary, form is the physical or informational embodiment; form is the instrument of function; value is benefit at cost; benefit derives from function and cost depends on form; abstractions hide unneeded details.

## Video 5 (05:56) — Identifying Entities

The second task: identify the entities, their function, and form. The relationship between the whole (the system) and the parts (the entities) is one of the most important aspects of system thinking — the system can be **decomposed** into entities, and entities can be **composed** into the whole. The brain naturally deals with complexity through decomposition (divide and conquer). Entities — the subdivision of the system — can be called subsystems, parts, modules, or elements; both the system and its entities have form and function and can be thought of as abstractions.

Decomposition is the top-down flow (dividing the system into smaller entities); composition is the bottom-up flow (combining modules into the system). Applying decomposition to team X: it is a group of people with the function of developing a new product, decomposed into a Design Team (function: designing the product) and a Production Team (function: manufacturing). Alternatively, team X could be formed by **composing** existing standing Design and Production teams, or a mixed case.

Entities are often represented in a **hierarchy**. Sometimes the hierarchy reflects form (rank or position — a full professor, an army officer); sometimes it reflects function (going top-down, functions become simpler). The hierarchy does not stop at two levels — the Design Team can decompose into Software and Hardware Design teams, and all of team X can be composed with other teams into the company's Product Development organization. Everything is a system; what you call "the system" is just a convenience for the problem you're working on.

(Guest commentary on software systems) In a software system it is important to understand how it decomposes into parts and the relationships between them — limiting the size of components so a human or team can understand each one, wrapping each component in a tight API so others have a simplified understanding at the boundaries, and maintaining a clear notion of hierarchy with an obvious top (graphical user interface), middle (business logic / middleware), and bottom (clear utilities). Structuring the system this way lets the organization function and limits bad emergence; where this breaks down is often where technical and business problems arise.

In summary: a system can be decomposed into entities and entities composed into the system — one of our most powerful tools for managing complexity; every system is part of a larger system and can be decomposed into smaller systems; every system and entity can be thought of as an abstraction with form and function; systems and their entities can be placed in a hierarchy based on form or function.

## Video 6 (06:30) — System Boundaries

Continuing the second task: an important step is defining the **system boundary**, which establishes the entities inside the system and the ones outside in the **context**. There is no single test for what is part of the system — entities could be highly interwoven, essential to the function, or supplied/controlled by you. Complexity scales with number, so a system thinker finds the minimum number of essential entities, keeping the number below the cognitive limit of **7 ± 2**.

For the top-down legged robot, decompose into the control system, the locomotion system, and the body (interwoven, essential, controlled by us). For the bottom-up supply chain, compose the procurer, the manufacturer, and the distributor (essential to deliver the function). What's inside the boundary is the scope of your work; what's outside will impact your work — always check the boundary because it may change as understanding grows.

Two approaches refine entities and boundaries: **holism** (thinking as widely as reasonable about what should be in the system, so you don't miss unknowns — possibly five to twelve entities) and **focus** (focusing on the entities truly important, keeping the total under five to seven; the focus list may change day to day). A good system thinker is always trying to reduce the number of entities — combining them and swapping them in and out.

Applying this to the legged robot: decompose into three entities (control system, locomotion system, body); think holistically about other impactful entities (the attached payload, a human operator, the ground the legs rest on, a provision for recharging the battery) — seven possible entities. Then focus: recharging is not important while operating; the details of the payload may not matter if performance is the motion at the attachment point — cutting to five entities. Then draw the boundary: for a build system, whatever is inside the boundary is what you deliver to the customer; the driver and the ground are outside the system but kept in the context. System thinkers use three categories: entities **in** the system; entities **in the context** (not in the system but whose presence fundamentally affects emergent function and performance); and entities **temporarily suppressed** (not in current thinking but possibly important to a different question later). Identify entities by decomposition or composition; limit to 7 ± 2; use holistic thinking then focus; draw a boundary dividing the system from its context.

## Video 7 (05:04) — Identifying Relationships

The third task: identify the relationships among the entities, their form and function. **Relationships are essential** — a system becomes a system because there are relationships; otherwise it is just a collection of independent entities. A group of people are a team when they work together; a robot is a set of interconnected entities, otherwise just a bag of parts.

Relationships have form and function. The **form** of relationships is how things are connected; the **function** is how things interact. Since emergence occurs in the functional domain, start with **functional relationships** — interactions. The interaction of the functions of the entities leads to emergence. Types of interactions: one entity **affects** another (the locomotion system moves the body); an operand is **passed** from one entity to another (goods moving through a supply chain); an operand is **shared** — two entities act jointly on the same operand (the product document the design and production teams co-develop).

Functional relationships can be within the system (within team X, the design-product function interacts with the manufacture-product function) or cross the boundary (in the supply chain, the transport-goods function interacts with the buy-goods function of the consumer outside the boundary). A new icon: a black arrow connecting entities' functions (white rectangles). The supply chain has five entities — three inside (Procurer, Manufacturer, Distributor) and two outside in the context (the Earth and the Consumer) — characterized by flows of material (input from the Earth, output to the Consumer) plus reverse flows of information. Team X has three internal functions (decision making, product design, product manufacturing) interacting with external functions (market analysis, operations planning); documents, goals, designs, and products are passed; the design and manufacturing functions share the same design document.

The legged robot (a cyber-physical system) has interactions in the information domain (instructions, commands) and the physical domain (exchange of loads). Some exchanges are **reciprocal** (the locomotion system places a load on the ground and the ground places the same load back); others are not (the driver issues instructions to the control system, but the control system issues no command back to the driver). In summary, a group of entities becomes a system when there are relationships; functional relationships have form and function; emergence happens as a direct result of functional relationships; functional relationships are interactions (one entity affects another, an operand is passed, or an operand is shared); and they can be among entities within the system or with entities outside in the context.

## Video 8 (06:49) — Structure

Continuing the third task: today we examine the **formal relationships between entities** — **structure**. Like the form of an entity, structure exists, was implemented, is more or less stable, and can be photographed. Structure is more tangible and observable than interaction. **Structure is what the relationships are; interaction is what the relationships do.** Imagine a choir: the singers interact by sharing notes and tempo; the seating chart just tells us where they sit — their structure. Structure is important because **it enables interaction** — it is the instrument of interaction. If there is a functional relationship between two entities, there is probably a formal structural relationship that enables it.

Examples: in the circulatory system, blood is transported from heart to lungs (interaction) by the veins and arteries (structure); a work of art is viewed because the art and the viewer are in the same space (structure). Structure is most commonly due to **connections** (electrical wires, fluidic pipes, data buses, mechanical connectors); **location** is another form (optical elements must be aligned; people can talk when in the same room); and **sequence** matters in software, poetry, and instructions. A new icon: a shaded arrow connecting the entities of form (shaded boxes).

The legged robot's structure includes an RF link, mechanical connectors, electrical wires, and physical contact with the ground — generally, where there is an interaction there is an element of structure: the RF link enables passing instructions; the electrical wires enable sharing commands and data. Structure is static; interactions are dynamic. In team X the structure is something of a platform — all members participate in meetings and have access to the same data links, enabling exchange of goals, directions, and design documents; there is often a symmetry in structure.

Especially careful system thinking is needed when relationships cross the system boundary — an **External Interface**, which has both form and function. Some interfaces are simple (plugging an appliance into a wall outlet); others involve great coordination and complexity (the interface where team X's production team delivers the product to the operations team) and may require standards, agreements, or a dedicated **Interface Manager**.

Thirty years ago, my colleagues and I at Orbital developed the **Pegasus rocket** — a small satellite launch vehicle launched from a high-flying airplane (boosting payload capacity and giving operational flexibility). Its challenges were twofold: the rocket itself was a complex system with new technology (an all-composite airframe, dozens of distributed microprocessors); and it was a system in a bigger sense — it had to safely mate with a large carrier aircraft, be compatible with the intended class of small satellites, and operate within existing air traffic and launch range infrastructure. Pegasus succeeded because those elements were understood from the beginning and harmonized. In summary, where there is a functional relationship there is usually a formal relationship; the functional interaction is usually enabled by a formal relationship; structure includes connections, location, and sequence; and when structure and interaction cross the system boundary, careful system thinking is required. Three of the four tasks of system thinking are now complete: identify the system, its function and form; identify the entities, their function and form; identify the relationships, their function and form.
