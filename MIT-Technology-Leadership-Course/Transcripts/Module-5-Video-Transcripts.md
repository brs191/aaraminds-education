## Video 1 (4:27) — Form, Function, and Performance

This part of the class looks at logistics and transportation systems — ways to coordinate the movement of people, goods, and services across a complex network. Systems are things that have entities and relationships — elements that are connected. Here we think about systems used to move things: physical things (materials, freight, people), transportation systems (cars, trains, airplanes), or even the movement of information (data packets across a communication network). The entities are interrelated; the entities and relationships enable a function; that function should be more powerful than the sum of the parts (emergence). We care about **performance** — how well the system conducts its functions.

The performance of a system depends on the individual entities, their relationships, our knowledge of the system, and our ability to regulate it. For an air transportation system, performance depends not just on the airports but on how they are connected, how well we know how they will behave, and our ability to manage flows. For a supply chain (supplier, manufacturer, distributor), performance depends on the entities, their relationships, what we know about those relationships, and our ability to regulate the system. Performance combines the benefit of supplying goods with the cost of setting up the supply chain — we want maximum benefit from the system as a whole. For a road network, the function is moving cars (the operand), the form is the road network and connectivity, the cost is the infrastructure investment, and the benefit is seamless movement, faster journey times, and decreased congestion and delays.

Two systems can have exactly the same function but very different performance — because the form is different, the entities are different, the scale or connectivity is different, or because our ability to control the entities, our knowledge of the system, and the behavior of the operands differ. The timescales on which aircraft, cars, and trains move differ, which affects our ability to control them and the performance we get.

## Video 2 (2:40) — Form, Function, and Performance: Three Scenarios

Systems can have the same function but very different performance. Three scenarios: (1) We know almost everything about the system — how individual entities behave, how they relate, and we can regulate both operand and entity behavior — so we can get very high performance through **optimization** (example: the Japanese rail system, where arrival/departure times are precise to within seconds and the average delay per train is typically under a minute). (2) We know something but not everything, with less ability to regulate — we can get better performance through **policies** (a stretch of road with cars: set rules of the road, time the traffic signals — an improvement over doing nothing, but not the first scenario's high performance). (3) We know very little — perhaps just the connectivity, and the operands act in their own self-interest — so we need to introduce **incentives** or provide **information/feedback** (a road network with no traffic lights to time: get improvement through congestion information or metering access). Each successive scenario yields less performance.

## Video 3 (4:07) — Counterintuitive Emergent Behavior

In complex systems the emergent behavior can be counterintuitive. We believe adding more resources always improves performance — e.g., adding a road to a congested network always decreases travel times — but adding a road in the wrong place can worsen performance and increase travel times. This is **Braess's paradox**, first introduced by German physicist Dietrich Braess in 1968.

Consider a road network with an origin and a destination and many cars. Two paths exist. Path through point A: a long, high-capacity highway (110 minutes from origin to A regardless of traffic) then a congested link whose travel time is C/10 minutes for C cars. Path through point B: a narrow road (C/10 minutes) then a high-capacity highway (110 minutes). With 1,000 cars, drivers see both paths as equivalent, so roughly 500 take each path; travel time on each is 110 + 500/10 = 160 minutes. Nobody changes paths — the system is at equilibrium.

If the city, unhappy with 160 minutes, adds a super-fast shortcut taking almost zero time from B to A (so people can go origin → B → A → destination), intuition says congestion should improve — but it does not. The next video shows why everybody's travel time becomes more than 160 minutes.

## Video 4 (5:22) — Examples of Counterintuitive Emergent Behavior

With the zero-time shortcut from B to A added: a car originally going origin → A might switch toward B. Now 501 cars go through B and 499 through A. The switching car's travel time becomes 501/10 (origin to B) + 0 (B to A) + 1,000/10 (the route to the destination shared by 1,000 cars) ≈ 150.1 minutes — saving almost 10 minutes. Seeing this, more drivers switch. Once 100 cars have switched, 600 cars use the northern route: their travel time is 600/10 + 1,000/10 = 160 minutes — the break-even point, no benefit. Meanwhile the 400 cars still going origin → A see 110 + 1,000/10 = 210 minutes. Almost everybody is worse off because they are acting selfishly: instead of all 1,000 cars seeing 160 minutes, 60% see 160 minutes and 40% see 210 minutes.

There is empirical evidence this happens. In 1990, during an Earth Day celebration in New York City, temporarily closing 42nd Street was expected to cause chaos — but it reduced congestion, because the original design led to selfish behavior that made the whole system worse. In Stuttgart, Germany in the 1960s, road infrastructure investment failed to improve traffic; congestion got worse until the newly built road was closed, after which traffic improved. There are parallels in electrical and mechanical systems. Braess's paradox is a sign of the **price of anarchy** — everybody acting in their own self-interest degrades the performance of the system as a whole. It teaches lessons about system design: connectivity matters; simply adding a connection made almost everyone worse off; and we may need to incentivize people to behave less in their self-interest and more for the system's benefit.

## Video 5 (9:42) — Queuing Systems

A **queue** is a useful abstraction for modeling systems with constrained or limited resources and obtaining insights on performance. Queuing theory has been used to analyze communication networks, computer networks, healthcare, factory floors, and essentially every logistics and transportation system. The simplest abstraction (a supermarket checkout): a customer/job arrives at the queue, waits, gets processed at the checkout counter (the **server**), and departs. Real systems may have several queues in series, a choice of queues, or many queues in parallel (e.g., an airport: check-in counter, security checkpoint, bag screening).

**Little's Law**, proposed in the 1950s by John Little, a professor at MIT: jobs arrive at a queue at rate λ; each person spends average time W in the system (waiting plus processing); the average number in the system is **L = λ × W**. This is a steady-state assumption (no transients, ramp-up, or ramp-down).

*Restaurant tomato inventory example:* tomatoes arrive at 1,000 pounds a week (λ); average inventory is 250 pounds (L). The shelf time W = L/λ = 250/1,000 = one-quarter of a week. Inventory turnover is 1/W = four times a week. Little's Law makes almost no assumptions beyond steady state.

*Traffic example:* for a one-km road link, λ is the traffic flow rate, W is the travel time (one km / velocity), and L is the average number of cars per km (traffic density). Little's Law gives traffic density = flow rate / velocity — empirically confirmed.

*Restaurant capacity example:* customers arrive at 30 per hour (λ), spending an average of 10 minutes (W = 1/6 hour). L = λW = 30/6 = 5 customers at any time. If an advertising blitz raises arrivals to 60 per hour with the same 10-minute stay, L becomes 10 customers — either the restaurant must be big enough for 10, or a second set of queues can cut the average time to 5 minutes (keeping L = 5 while supporting twice the arrivals). Little's Law helps us understand systems — simple queues or complex networks — relating arrival rate, average time in system, and system size (inventory, restaurant size, cash on hand, etc.).

## Video 6 (4:48) — Networks

Many logistics and transportation systems have a large number of interconnected elements (hundreds of airports in a country's air transportation system; millions of nodes on the Internet). **Graphs or network models** are a powerful abstraction: **nodes** represent entities (facilities, warehouses, computers, airports, bus stations), and **edges** represent interactions between entities.

As Braess's paradox showed, emergent and counterintuitive behavior can occur with interconnected elements. The **propagation of disturbances** is a challenge — a failure is no longer restricted to one node but can spread to the entire system. **Cascading failures** are an emergent property of network systems. In 2016, Southwest had a computer outage at its Dallas Love hub (about an hour) and Delta had a power outage at its Atlanta hub (about five hours). Although each event affected a single node briefly, problems spread system-wide — passengers delayed, aircraft and crews in the wrong places — taking days to recover, and each disturbance cost over $150 million because the failure could not be contained to one node.

A software system can also be a large network of entities and relationships (structural or functional, passing function, control, or data). In one case, a video game company's platform had unrecognized dependency structures between games not managed in the architecture — a local performance improvement in one game, once integrated and propagated, caused bugs that nearly made other teams miss their Christmas releases. Interconnectivity is good (the system collectively does more) but means problems and failures are no longer connected to one place.

## Video 7 (5:19) — The Bullwhip Effect

Consider interconnected entities making decisions in response to events around them. Driving down a freeway, when the car in front slows and you see brake lights, you slow down — and the car behind you slows too. To be safe, given delay in response time and uncertainty, each driver adds a **safety buffer** — slowing a little more. The car behind slows even more, and so on. This creates a trade-off between efficiency and uncertainty: more uncertainty leads to bigger buffers — safer, but a loss of efficiency.

In a supply chain this is the **Bullwhip Effect**. Final customer demand at a retail store varies a little. If each entity looks only one step downstream and reacts to the demand it sees there (like the car reacting to the one in front), adding a buffer for uncertainty, then small variabilities in final customer demand lead to larger and larger variability and buffers upstream — finally producing very large inventory levels in response to small fluctuations in customer demand. In a tight-supply environment, each salesperson tries to protect their customers by ordering more — ordering three times the need expecting a third — and demand appears to go through the roof. Volvo's example: a sales campaign to boost eco-friendly cars raised dealership sales (mainly clearing inventory); distributors saw the sales increase and manufactured more, building up inventory. Without wide information sharing — only reacting one step downstream — you get large inventory levels and huge variability. The counter is **better information sharing**: if everyone knew actual customer demand at the point of sale, they would not be conservative — like knowing the intent of cars two or three ahead of you in traffic.

## Video 8 (3:57) — Modern Logistics and Transportation Systems

Modern logistics and transportation systems raise the question of the role of **AI and machine learning**. One way to think about it: the ability to go from data (now available in large amounts) to **models** to **decisions and insights** — changing how we operate and manage systems.

In scenario one (we understand the system well and can regulate it), optimization algorithms can be built because data-driven models of higher resolution enable fine-grained regulation with very high performance; AI also enables much higher levels of autonomy, reducing variability and improving control. The second role comes from increased **information sharing**: uncertainty and variability cause inefficiency because we add buffers — countered by sharing and transparency (e.g., providing visibility of endpoint sales to everyone, equivalent to broadcasting speed reductions across a road network, or providing traffic congestion information). In the third scenario (little control over entities/operands), improved decision making with data and AI helps **design incentives** to nudge the system toward desired behavior (congestion pricing to shift travel to off-peak times, tolling mechanisms, incentives to share information). As incentives to share information increase, **privacy and security** concerns arise — there is a trade-off between information sharing and privacy. Performance has many measures (efficiency, security, privacy) with trade-offs that intelligent systems learn to balance.

## Video 9 (4:30) — The Computational Approach to System Thinking

System thinking is problem solving in complex settings. A system is an entity with interrelated and interdependent parts, defined by its modules, the boundaries between modules, and their interaction; it is more than the sum of its parts. Changing one part affects other parts and generally the whole. The global behavior depends on local functions and interactions — so systems can be thought of as hierarchically composed of subsystems with increasingly simple function toward the bottom of the hierarchy.

Example: imagine a world where anyone can use systems thinking to reason conceptually about a complex device they want to create and get automatic support to realize it. Our user, **Alice**, wants to invent a device to entertain her cat while she's at work. She heads to a new store, "24-hour robot manufacturing," explores the design space through an intuitive interface, settles on a design, and the store prints the device immediately for not much cost.

Suppose you want a robot to play chess with you. From this high-level, conceptual specification, computational tools can **parse** the specification to determine the required behaviors (picking up a piece, dropping it, moving it without bumping others), **identify** mechanisms that realize those behaviors, and **assemble** them — a non-expert designs a chess-playing robot in about two hours using modularity, hierarchy, and composition. Implementing this approach needs several modules: a **database** of existing designs (with appropriate representations); a **segmentation and search** process to choose the correct modules (what should be composed, and how); a way to **assemble** them; a way to **simulate and verify** that the system meets the specification; and finally **fabrication** by rapid prototyping.

## Video 10 (5:15) — The Robot Compiler

The case study is the **Robot Compiler** — a compiler takes a high-level specification and delivers a working system. The user conceives an idea (an insect-shaped device that plays with a cat or plays chess). An insect-shaped machine needs a body, legs, and a claw system to pick up and drop pieces. With a database of all possible bodies, legs, and claws, you could search it to create the desired system. The library might include expandable bodies, a two-finger claw, and different legged structures. From these designs you identify individual functionality and compose them — e.g., a telescoping locomotion system composed with a claw system to create a new design (a telescoping arm with three or four fingers). If you have a database of designs, you can segment and compose it to make new designs — requiring that designs are segmentable, composable, and **parameterized** (to achieve big and little claws, big and little expansions, at all scales).

The design process starts with a database of parts, enables interactive modeling for the user (who employs system thinking at the conceptual level), and once finalized, uses rapid prototyping. Simulation engines let the user manipulate components: take a body and adjust its size, attach different legs — done conceptually. Design is done **by folding**: the user thinks in 3D and the system automatically creates a 2D representation. The tool lets the user select high-level motions (e.g., locomotion) and adjust parameters (how quickly the robot moves, payload, form factor). The body is then printed using 3D printing and electronic components assembled.

Rapidly creating systems with complex functionality involves a software and a hardware component, each package-abstracted and databased. This work draws on creating modular robot systems and hierarchical systems on one side, and computational design and rapid fabrication on the other — computational design for complex systems fits between them, taking function from modular hierarchical machines and customized fabrication from computational design and fabrication. The challenges: creating the database of components (the data-driven approach relies on a database seeded with low-level designs and some expert-created higher-level designs); a process to choose and compose the modules; verification that the result meets the specifications; and fabrication.

## Video 11 (4:38) — The Database

The entities in the database are mechanical parts, electromechanical parts, and software components, all **parameterized** for large representation and scalability. For designing conceptually in 3D and fabricating by printing, every component, assembly, and sub-assembly needs a 3D representation and a corresponding unfolded 2D representation. A new design's components are considered **hierarchically** — for a walking machine, the root is the complete walker, then the body and the legs, with legs subdivided into front and back legs; the hierarchy nodes correspond to actual library components that can be instantiated and mapped onto physical entities.

Beyond the hierarchy, we need to know how components **interact** — physically (where the legs connect to the body) and functionally (what degrees of freedom each connection allows). These motion constraints have implications for the control code needed. Every component is parameterized (different leg lengths, body widths, system heights); 3D parameters have corresponding 2D parameters so manipulations in 3D are reflected in 2D. **Constraints** are either functional (the legs must be longer than the body or the robot does not move) or capture local requirements (where a leg must connect to the body). The user can pick parameters by trial and error, or the parameter manipulation can be formulated as an **optimization problem** searching for the best parameters for the specified performance — abstracting away the manual search. The gap between symbolic reasoning and concrete precise specifications is filled through computational approaches.

## Video 12 (2:16) — Composing the Modules

How do we compose the modules? Placing a new part involves identifying its precise dimensions and the precise location of contact. As with the manipulation algorithm, **optimization** computes these parameters precisely. Connectivity information from the database ensures added parts connect to the new model the same way as the database example. When adding legs to the body, the user specifies an approximate location, and the optimization engine finds the precise location with minimum deviation from the user input while enforcing the constraints and specification (the cost function minimizes changes from the user specification). The user can keep manipulating the part until satisfied with the snap configuration. These operations create the hierarchical structure of the working model and guarantee it has the same representation as the database models, while adding the constraints needed for functionality. With a small library of body parts, wheels, legs, and claws, a whole suite of machines can be created.

## Video 13 (4:20) — Simulate, Verify, and Fabricate

Given a design, how do we know it meets the requirement? Connect the design tools to **simulation tools** that verify the dynamics of the desired function. For a walking machine, check in simulation whether the design is stable, moves fast enough, and supports the desired payload range. **Stability** is measured by where the center of mass is relative to the system during motion — if it falls outside the supporting legs, the body or legs must be adjusted so the center of mass stays inside the stability region. Stability feedback guides the user to modify the design until it meets the specifications — systems thinking, because the user thinks conceptually about complex movement while computation ensures constraints and requirements are met.

Once simulated and shown to meet the high-level specifications, the design is fabricated by **print-and-fold** techniques: the 2D unfolded version was computed automatically after every user motion; body fabrication is done by 3D printing (with hinges and connectors that make folding easy); the final design is sent to a 3D printer and within minutes there is a working prototype. A similar approach creates the **control system** that enables the robot to walk: start with a database of electronic and electro-mechanical parts; the user roughly specifies what is needed (a motor for the left legs, a motor for the right legs); the system automatically synthesizes the electronics substrate to deliver the controlled motions. Throughout — database, user interaction, simulation and verification — the process produces the mechanical design, a wiring diagram, and wiring instructions for assembling the final device.

## Video 14 (3:26) — Computational Design

Summary of computational design for complex systems: the process starts with a **concept**, mapped into **components** from the database (legs, bodies, a manipulator, a computer, motors, sensors, supporting infrastructure). For each design the approach produces the mechanical design files, the electronic design files, and the assembly instructions. The mechanical design can go to a regular printer (a paper copy to assemble), a 3D printer, or a laser cutter. Once the electro-mechanical components are assembled, the **software infrastructure** delivers the function — a similar compositional approach synthesizes controllers automatically for every sensor and motor. The result **co-designs** the mechanical part of the robot, the computational substrate, and the programming infrastructure — beginning with a high-level function and synthesizing a functional working machine.

This approach is universal — analogous to the **app store**, which has democratized computation (for any computational task, there may be an app to realize it). The goal is a universal, democratized approach letting anyone employ systems thinking with computational tools — thinking about a system functionally, symbolically, conceptually, and letting the system figure out the details from dream to realized device. The process is enabled by three concepts: **data-driven specification, hierarchical composition, and simulation and verification.** With these tools, Alice's dream of walking into a store and synthesizing a customized playmate for her cat is likely to become reality — yielding a world of systems thinkers who can create any object they want from functional specifications.
